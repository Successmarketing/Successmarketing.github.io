50 Types of Food Packaging Products Every Business Should Know

February 10, 2025 22 min read How-To Guide

Walk into any food business -- a street-side chaat counter in Kota, a fine-dining restaurant in Mumbai, a catering service in Jaipur, or a cloud kitchen in Bangalore -- and you will find packaging products everywhere. Each product serves a specific purpose, and choosing the wrong one can ruin food presentation, increase costs, or even violate food safety regulations.

After more than three decades of supplying disposable food packaging to businesses across India, we have put together this definitive reference list. These are the 50 packaging product types that matter most, organised by category, with practical details on what each one does and where it fits in your operation.

Containers and Boxes (1-12)

1. Round Food Containers

The workhorse of food delivery packaging. Available in capacities from 100ml to 1500ml, round containers with snap-fit lids work for everything from chutneys and raita to full curries. Most food businesses in India stock at least three sizes. Polypropylene (PP) containers are microwave-safe and hold up well against oily gravies, while newer bagasse versions offer an eco-friendly alternative.

2. Rectangular Meal Containers

Rectangular containers make more efficient use of delivery bags than round ones. They are ideal for rice-based meals, biryanis, and combo meals where you need volume. The 500ml and 750ml sizes dominate the market for single-serving deliveries. Look for containers with tight-fitting lids to prevent spills during last-mile delivery.

3. Compartment Containers

Also called sectioned trays or thali containers, these have two, three, or five divisions to keep different food items separate. Essential for tiffin services, corporate catering, and any business serving complete meals. The five-compartment version mimics a traditional Indian thali, keeping dal, sabzi, rice, roti, and pickle in their own sections.

4. Clamshell Containers

A hinged container that opens and closes like a clamshell. Widely used for burgers, sandwiches, momos, and snack items. The single-piece design means no separate lid to misplace, which speeds up packing in high-volume kitchens. Available in polystyrene, PP, and bagasse.

5. Aluminium Foil Containers

Unbeatable for hot food that needs to retain heat during delivery. Aluminium containers are oven-safe, can handle temperatures above 300 degrees Celsius, and provide an excellent moisture barrier. Popular for biryani, kebabs, and tandoori items. They come in round, rectangular, and oval shapes. The main drawback is that they cannot go into a microwave.

6. Paper Meal Boxes

Kraft paper boxes with a food-grade inner coating have become the default for many delivery-focused restaurants. They are lightweight, stackable, and print well for branding. Common in Chinese and continental food service. Sizes range from 500ml snack boxes to 1200ml meal boxes.

7. Noodle Boxes

The distinctive wire-handle box associated with Chinese takeaway food. These flat-packed boxes assemble quickly, hold shape well, and provide decent heat retention. They work for noodles, fried rice, and manchurian-style dishes. Available in 16oz, 26oz, and 32oz sizes.

8. Burger Boxes

Specifically designed to hold burgers without crushing them. The box dimensions match standard burger sizes, preventing the patty from sliding around during transport. Corrugated versions add insulation. Most QSR chains in India use custom-printed burger boxes for brand visibility.

9. Cake Boxes

Sturdy corrugated or duplex board boxes with transparent windows that let customers see the product without opening the box. Cake boxes come in sizes from 250g pastry boxes to 5kg celebration cake boxes, and always include a base board to support the product.

10. Pizza Boxes

Corrugated cardboard boxes that balance heat retention with steam venting. Standard sizes in India are 7-inch (personal), 10-inch (medium), and 12-inch (large). The fluted cardboard structure insulates the pizza while small vent holes prevent the crust from getting soggy. Most pizza businesses use printed boxes as a major branding tool.

11. Sweet Boxes

Decorative boxes designed specifically for Indian sweets and mithai. Available in everything from simple 250g cardboard boxes to ornate festival gift boxes with metallic finishes. Sweet shops typically stock 250g, 500g, and 1kg sizes year-round, adding premium options before Diwali, Raksha Bandhan, and wedding seasons.

12. Dry Fruit Boxes

Premium packaging for dry fruits and nuts, often featuring compartments, magnetic closures, and satin or velvet lining. These are as much about presentation as protection. The gift-giving culture in India drives massive demand during festivals and wedding seasons.

Cups and Glasses (13-20)

13. Paper Cups (Hot Beverages)

Single-wall and double-wall paper cups for tea, coffee, and other hot drinks. The inner PE or PLA coating prevents leakage. Size selection is critical -- 60ml and 80ml for cutting chai, 150ml for standard tea, 200ml for coffee, and 300ml for large servings. Double-wall cups eliminate the need for sleeves.

14. Paper Cups (Cold Beverages)

Similar to hot cups but with coatings optimised for condensation resistance. Used for lassi, buttermilk, cold coffee, and soft drinks. The coating prevents the cup from weakening when cold liquid causes moisture to form on the outer surface. Dome lids and flat lids are available separately.

15. Plastic Glasses (PET/PP)

Transparent plastic glasses used primarily for juices, smoothies, and cold beverages where visual appeal matters. PET glasses are crystal-clear and show off colourful drinks beautifully. PP versions are more heat-resistant. Available from 200ml to 700ml.

16. Foam Cups

Expanded polystyrene cups that provide excellent insulation. Note that many Indian states have banned thermocol products, so check local regulations before stocking. Where permitted, they remain popular for their low cost and insulating properties.

17. Ice Cream Cups

Small paper cups, typically 50ml to 200ml, with a wax or PE coating that handles frozen temperatures without becoming soggy. Ice cream cups need to withstand freezer storage and still feel comfortable in the customer's hand. Usually paired with flat lids and small wooden spoons.

18. Soup Cups

Deep, sturdy paper or PP cups in 200ml to 500ml sizes with vented lids. The taller profile and heavier construction prevent spills when transporting hot soup. Many restaurants also use them for dal, rasam, and sambar servings in meal combos.

19. Sauce Cups and Portion Cups

Tiny containers -- 25ml, 30ml, or 50ml -- for condiments, dips, and sauces. Every delivery order of momos, rolls, or fried snacks needs these. They come in PP plastic with snap lids. Some businesses use small aluminium foil cups as a premium alternative.

20. Kulhar (Clay Cups)

Traditional unglazed clay cups that add an earthy flavour to tea and kulfi. Kulhars are making a comeback in railway stations, heritage restaurants, and businesses wanting a distinctly Indian presentation. They are fully biodegradable and disposable.

Plates, Bowls, and Trays (21-28)

21. Round Plates

The most common disposable plate in India. Available in 6-inch, 7-inch, 9-inch, and 12-inch sizes. Materials include paper, bagasse, areca leaf, and plastic. The 9-inch size handles a full meal, while 6-inch works for snacks and starters. For catering events, the 12-inch plate serves as a thali base.

22. Compartment Plates

Plates with moulded dividers that keep food items separate. The five-compartment plate is essentially a disposable thali and dominates wedding catering, langar events, and institutional feeding programmes across India.

23. Square and Rectangular Plates

A more contemporary look compared to round plates. Used by restaurants and caterers who want a modern presentation. Square plates in 6-inch and 8-inch sizes work well for starters, chaat, and individual dessert portions.

24. Bowls

Available from 100ml dessert bowls to 750ml serving bowls. Disposable bowls are essential for curries, dals, soups, ice cream, and raita. Paper and bagasse bowls are the most popular materials. Ensure the bowl you choose can handle the temperature and oil content of your specific dishes.

25. Dona (Leaf Bowls)

Traditional pressed-leaf bowls used widely at street food stalls, temple prasad distribution, and community feasts. Made from sal or banyan leaves stitched with small sticks. Donas are the original zero-waste packaging -- fully natural and completely biodegradable. They remain the cheapest bowl option in the market.

26. Food Trays

Serving trays in paper, foam, or bagasse for presenting multiple items together. Fast-food restaurants use them to serve fries, burgers, and drinks as a combo. Catering services use larger trays for buffet service. Standard sizes range from small snack trays to full meal trays.

27. Pav Bhaji Plates

A specialty plate with a deeper well on one side for the bhaji and a flat section for pav. Popular with street food vendors and restaurants serving Mumbai-style pav bhaji. This seemingly small design detail makes a real difference in how cleanly the dish can be eaten.

28. Idli/Dosa Plates

Elongated plates or trays designed to hold dosas without folding them. South Indian restaurants and tiffin centres use these specialised plates to maintain the presentation quality of masala dosas and uttapams during delivery.

Wrapping and Covering Materials (29-35)

29. Aluminium Foil Rolls

Versatile wrapping material for rotis, parathas, kebabs, sandwiches, and shawarmas. Available in 9-metre to 75-metre rolls and in various widths. Aluminium foil provides an excellent heat and moisture barrier. Most restaurants in India keep foil rolls in the kitchen for wrapping and covering hot food.

30. Cling Film

PVC or LDPE film that clings to itself and to container rims. Used to cover bowls, wrap cut fruits, seal containers, and protect food during storage. Available in 100-metre to 500-metre rolls. Food-grade cling film must be BPA-free and FSSAI-compliant for direct food contact.

31. Butter Paper

Grease-resistant paper used to wrap sandwiches, line baking trays, and separate food layers in storage. It prevents oil from seeping through while allowing the food to breathe. Bakeries and burger joints go through large quantities of butter paper daily.

32. Wax Paper

Paper coated with food-grade paraffin wax. It provides better moisture resistance than plain butter paper and is used for wrapping cheese, cold sandwiches, and sweets. Not heat-safe, so it should not go into ovens or microwaves.

33. Food Wrapping Paper

Printed or plain paper used to wrap street food items like vada pav, samosas, and kathi rolls. Often printed with the business name and contact details for branding. Grease-proof wrapping paper with a food-safe coating is the right choice for oily items.

34. Shrink Wrap

Plastic film that shrinks tightly around products when heat is applied. Used primarily for retail packaging of water bottles, snack bundles, and multi-pack items. Requires a heat gun or shrink tunnel. Important for businesses packaging retail food products for distribution.

35. Stretch Film

Highly stretchable film used to wrap pallets and secure bulk shipments of food products. This is a logistics packaging product rather than food-contact packaging. Essential for any food business that ships in bulk or manages warehouse inventory.

Cutlery and Accessories (36-42)

36. Disposable Spoons

Available in plastic (PP/PS), wooden, bamboo, and corn starch materials. Spoon selection depends on the food type -- lightweight spoons work for desserts and ice cream, while heavier-duty spoons are needed for rice and thick curries. With the plastic ban in effect, wooden and bamboo spoons are gaining market share rapidly.

37. Disposable Forks

Used primarily in continental and Chinese food service. Less common in traditional Indian food settings where spoons dominate, but essential for businesses serving pasta, noodles, salads, and Western-style dishes. Available in the same material options as spoons.

38. Disposable Knives

Serrated disposable knives for cutting sandwiches, cakes, and bread items. Not commonly required in Indian food service but standard in bakeries, Western-style cafes, and QSR outlets. Usually sold as part of cutlery kits with spoon and fork.

39. Chopsticks

Bamboo or wooden chopsticks packaged in paper sleeves. Required by Chinese and Asian fusion restaurants. Typically sold in boxes of 100 pairs. Round chopsticks are easier to manufacture, but squared-off tips provide better grip on noodles.

40. Straws

Paper, bamboo, wheat, and rice straws have replaced plastic straws following the single-use plastic ban. Paper straws in 6mm and 8mm diameters handle most beverages. The 8mm size is necessary for thick drinks like smoothies and milkshakes. The switch from plastic to paper straws has been one of the most visible changes in Indian food service.

41. Stirrers

Small wooden or bamboo sticks used to stir tea, coffee, and cold beverages. Typically 10cm to 14cm long. Plastic stirrers are banned under the 2022 rules, making wooden stirrers the standard. Some premium cafes use bamboo stirrers for a better aesthetic.

42. Toothpicks

Bamboo or birch wood toothpicks, individually wrapped or in bulk dispensers. A small but expected accompaniment at restaurants. Mint-flavoured toothpicks add a finishing touch that customers notice. Available in round and flat profiles.

Bags and Carry Solutions (43-47)

43. Paper Carry Bags

Kraft paper bags with twisted or flat handles for carrying packaged food. Carry bags are a major branding opportunity since customers carry them visibly. Available in brown kraft and white kraft, in sizes from small (for single items) to jumbo (for large catering orders). Minimum 80 GSM for food use.

44. Non-Woven Bags

Reusable bags made from polypropylene fibre. These are not disposable in the traditional sense but are used by many food businesses as a premium alternative to paper bags. They print well, hold more weight, and customers tend to reuse them, extending your brand visibility.

45. Zip-Lock Pouches

Resealable plastic pouches used for dry snacks, spice mixes, pickles, and portion-packed ingredients. Available in various sizes and thicknesses. Food-grade zip-lock pouches must meet FSSAI requirements for direct food contact.

46. Vacuum Pouches

Multi-layer laminated pouches designed for vacuum sealing. Used for extending the shelf life of ready-to-eat meals, marinades, paneer, and meat products. Require a vacuum sealing machine. These are critical for any business doing meal prep or retail food distribution.

47. Tamper-Evident Bags

Delivery bags with adhesive seals that show clear evidence of opening. These have become standard for food delivery since aggregators like Swiggy and Zomato started prioritising hygiene packaging. The seal reassures customers that their food has not been accessed during transit.

Specialty and Support Items (48-50)

48. Cup Sleeves and Holders

Corrugated paper sleeves that slip over hot beverage cups to protect the customer's hand. Cup sleeves are cheaper than double-wall cups and serve as additional branding space. Cafes and tea chains use custom-printed sleeves for marketing.

49. Tissue Paper and Napkins

Available as paper napkins (1-ply and 2-ply), wet wipes, and dispenser tissues. Every food business needs napkins -- the question is which type and quality level. Fine-dining uses 2-ply embossed napkins, while street food vendors use basic 1-ply. Wet wipes in individual sachets have become popular in delivery orders.

50. Food Labels and Stickers

Self-adhesive labels for sealing containers, displaying nutritional information, and branding. Tamper-evident labels double as security seals on delivery containers. Thermal labels work with small printers for date coding and batch marking. Every packaged food product leaving your kitchen should carry proper labelling.

How to Choose the Right Products for Your Business

With 50 product types available, the selection process can be overwhelming. Here is a practical framework based on business type:

For delivery-focused cloud kitchens: Prioritise leak-proof containers (round and rectangular), compartment containers, paper bags, tamper-evident seals, and portion cups. Invest in containers that stack well to optimise storage space.

For dine-in restaurants: Focus on plates, bowls, cups, cutlery, and napkins. Choose materials that match your restaurant's positioning -- areca leaf for premium, bagasse for mid-range, paper for budget-friendly.

For catering businesses: Stock compartment plates, large serving trays, aluminium foil containers, heavy-duty bowls, and bulk-quantity carry bags. Buy in large wholesale lots to get the best per-unit prices.

For bakeries: Cake boxes, pastry containers, butter paper, cupcake inserts, and printed carry bags are your essentials. Invest in attractive packaging since bakery products are often purchased as gifts.

For street food vendors: Donas, wrapping paper, small paper plates, sauce cups, and paper bags. Keep it simple, affordable, and compliant with local regulations.

Buying Smart: Wholesale vs Retail

The difference between wholesale and retail pricing on food packaging products is substantial -- often 30-50% on high-volume items. A restaurant buying 250ml paper cups retail might pay Rs 2.50 per cup, while the wholesale price for a case of 2,000 drops to Rs 1.40-1.60 per cup. Over a year, that difference adds up to lakhs of rupees for a busy food business.

The key to smart procurement is understanding your consumption patterns, stocking the right quantities, and working with a supplier who maintains consistent quality across all 50 product categories. A single-source wholesale partner simplifies your supply chain, ensures material compatibility, and gives you better negotiating leverage.
