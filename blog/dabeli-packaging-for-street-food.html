Dabeli Packaging for Street Food Vendors and Restaurants in India

June 20, 2025 12 min read Food Packaging

Dabeli, the spiced potato-filled bun that originated in the Kutch region of Gujarat, has quietly become one of India's most popular street foods. What started as a regional snack sold for a few rupees at roadside stalls in Mandvi and Bhuj has spread across the country, finding devoted fans in Mumbai, Pune, Ahmedabad, Surat, and increasingly in cities far beyond Gujarat's borders. In Mumbai alone, dabeli is estimated to be sold at thousands of street stalls, competing directly with the city's iconic vada pav for the title of the most consumed street snack.

The appeal of dabeli lies in its layered complexity. A single dabeli packs an extraordinary range of flavours and textures into a small, affordable package: the soft pav bun, the spiced potato filling seasoned with dabeli masala, the crunch of roasted peanuts and pomegranate seeds, the sweetness of tamarind chutney, the heat of garlic chutney, and the fine sev sprinkled on top. It is a masterclass in street food engineering, and at Rs 15 to Rs 30 per piece, it offers remarkable value.

But dabeli's very complexity creates packaging challenges. The filling is moist and can make the pav soggy over time. The chutneys leak if not contained properly. The sev loses its crunch the moment it contacts moisture. And the overall assembly is soft and prone to compression if squeezed in a bag or container. Getting the packaging right is essential for any dabeli business that serves takeaway or delivery orders.

How Street Vendors Currently Package Dabeli

At a traditional dabeli stall, the packaging is minimal and functional. The vendor assembles the dabeli, wraps it loosely in a small sheet of paper, and hands it to the customer. The customer eats it immediately, standing at the stall, often ordering a second or third one as they finish the first. In this scenario, packaging barely matters because the product is consumed within seconds of preparation.

Some vendors use butter paper or wax paper for wrapping, which provides a grease-resistant surface that keeps the customer's hands clean. Others use small paper bags into which the dabeli is placed. A few stalls in Mumbai and Ahmedabad have started using branded printed paper wraps that serve as both packaging and marketing, with the stall's name, phone number, and sometimes a Swiggy or Zomato listing printed on the wrap.

The shift from plain wrapping to branded packaging costs very little, perhaps Rs 0.50 to Rs 1.00 more per piece, but the branding impact is substantial. A customer carrying a branded dabeli wrap through a market or office is a walking advertisement.

Packaging Options for Dabeli

Butter Paper and Grease-Proof Paper Wraps

For eat-immediately service, a sheet of food-grade butter paper or grease-proof paper is the ideal dabeli packaging. It is inexpensive at Rs 0.30 to Rs 0.60 per sheet, provides a clean wrapping surface, absorbs minor grease without becoming translucent, and can be custom printed with your branding. The paper should be large enough to wrap the entire dabeli with some overlap, typically a 25 x 25 cm sheet for a standard-sized dabeli.

Aluminium Foil Wraps

Aluminium foil wraps offer superior heat retention compared to paper. A dabeli wrapped in foil stays warm for 15 to 20 minutes, which is useful for takeaway customers who are carrying the food to a nearby office or home. Foil also provides a complete moisture barrier, preventing any chutney or filling moisture from reaching the customer's hands.

The downside of foil for dabeli is that it traps steam, which can soften the pav over extended periods. For short-duration takeaway of up to 15 minutes, this is not a problem. For longer delivery windows, foil alone is not sufficient.

Paper Bags and Pouches

A food-grade kraft paper bag is a step up from a simple wrap. The bag contains the dabeli securely, provides space for branding on the exterior, and is easy for the customer to carry. For stalls that sell dabeli in pairs or sets of four, a slightly larger bag holds multiple dabeils without crushing them.

Line the bag with butter paper or a grease-resistant liner to prevent oil stains from showing through the exterior. A stained, translucent bag looks unappealing and undermines the perceived quality of the food inside.

Clamshell Boxes for Delivery

For delivery orders through Swiggy, Zomato, or direct ordering, a small clamshell container or a cardboard box provides the structural protection that wraps and bags cannot offer. A clamshell prevents the dabeli from being crushed during the delivery ride, keeps the chutneys from leaking onto the delivery bag, and presents a professional appearance when the customer opens the order.

For delivery, consider packing the sev separately in a small sealed pouch or container. Sev is the most moisture-sensitive component of a dabeli, and keeping it separate allows the customer to sprinkle it on fresh at the time of eating, preserving the crunch that makes dabeli special.

The Sev Problem: Keeping Crunch in Delivery

Sev, the thin crispy noodle topping, is what gives dabeli its textural contrast. Without crunchy sev, a dabeli is just a soft potato sandwich. But sev is hygroscopic, meaning it absorbs moisture from the air and from any wet surface it touches. In a sealed delivery container, the moisture from the chutneys and the warm potato filling creates an environment that softens sev within minutes.

The solution is simple: pack the sev separately. A small paper pouch or a sealed cup containing a portion of sev allows the customer to add it to the dabeli at the moment of eating. This adds one extra container to the packaging, costing Rs 0.50 to Rs 1.00, but it preserves the crunch that defines the dabeli experience. Brands that skip this step and send pre-assembled dabeli with sev already on top consistently receive complaints about soggy, flat-tasting products.

Dabeli Packaging for Events and Catering

Dabeli is a popular catering choice for weddings, corporate events, school functions, and birthday parties across Gujarat, Maharashtra, and Rajasthan. For event catering, the packaging approach depends on the service format.

Live counter: A dabeli counter at an event where the vendor prepares dabelis fresh for each guest needs only butter paper wraps and napkins. The dabeli is assembled, wrapped, and handed directly to the guest. Keep packaging minimal and focus on speed of service.

Pre-packed boxes: For events where food is distributed in individual boxes or meal trays, pack each dabeli in a butter paper wrap inside a small section of a compartment meal box. Include a small pouch of sev on the side. This works for corporate lunches, train journey meals, and school event distributions.

Bulk transport: When transporting large quantities of dabeli from a kitchen to an event venue, stack individually wrapped dabelis in large cardboard boxes lined with butter paper. Separate layers with a sheet of paper to prevent sticking. Transport the sev and extra chutneys in separate sealed containers and add them at the venue just before serving.

Building a Dabeli Brand Through Packaging

In cities like Mumbai, Ahmedabad, and Surat, where dabeli competition is intense, packaging is a key brand differentiator. The most successful dabeli stalls and chains have invested in distinctive packaging that customers recognise and remember.

Effective branding elements for dabeli packaging include your stall or brand name prominently displayed, a consistent colour scheme that matches your stall signage, your phone number for direct orders, your listing information for delivery apps, and your FSSAI license number for regulatory compliance and customer trust. Custom-printed butter paper wraps and paper bags are available at minimum orders of 5,000 to 10,000 pieces from most printing suppliers, and the per-unit cost premium over plain packaging is typically Rs 0.20 to Rs 0.50.

Hygiene and Regulatory Compliance

Dabeli stalls have faced increasing scrutiny from food safety authorities in recent years, particularly around packaging materials. The use of newspaper for wrapping food, once common across Indian street food, is now explicitly banned under FSSAI regulations. Newspaper ink contains harmful chemicals that migrate into food, particularly hot and moist food like dabeli.

Switch to food-grade butter paper, grease-proof paper, or aluminium foil. The cost difference is marginal, typically Rs 0.20 to Rs 0.50 more per serving, but the health and regulatory benefits are significant. Display your FSSAI registration at your stall and on your packaging to demonstrate compliance and build customer confidence.

For delivery operations, ensure all containers are food-grade certified. Reputable wholesale packaging suppliers provide certification documentation with their products. Keep these certificates accessible in case of an inspection.
