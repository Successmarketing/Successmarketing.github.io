Food Packaging Trends in Kerala: Where Tradition Meets Sustainability

August 22, 2025 14 min read Industry

Kerala occupies a unique position in India's food packaging landscape. It is a state where environmental awareness runs high, literacy rates are the best in the country, and consumers actively question the materials their food comes in. It is also a state with a food culture that is coconut-oil-rich, fish-heavy, and served in generous portions that test the limits of any container. The Malayali approach to food packaging reflects this combination: there is a genuine push towards sustainable alternatives, but no willingness to compromise on the functional requirements that Kerala's cuisine demands.

The state's food packaging market is estimated at Rs 2,200-2,600 crore annually. This is smaller than Maharashtra or Tamil Nadu in absolute terms, but Kerala's per-capita packaging consumption is among the highest in India because of its high urbanisation rate (nearly 48% of the population lives in urban areas), a strong food-away-from-home culture driven by the Gulf remittance economy, and one of the densest networks of restaurants and tea shops per capita in the country. An average Keralite spends significantly more on eating out than the national average, and every meal consumed outside the home involves packaging at some stage.

The Kerala Meals: Packaging a Feast

The Kerala sadya, the full vegetarian feast traditionally served on a banana leaf, is one of the most elaborate meal formats in India. Even the everyday Kerala meals served in restaurants involves rice (typically two servings), sambar, rasam, two or three vegetable thorans and curries, pickle, papadam, payasam, and sometimes a fish or meat curry as well. Packaging this for delivery is a logistical challenge that Kerala's food businesses have spent years solving.

The banana leaf itself is the original sustainable packaging. It is naturally produced, fully biodegradable, adds a subtle flavour to food, and requires no manufacturing process beyond harvesting and cleaning. For dine-in service, nothing has improved upon it in thousands of years. But for delivery and takeaway, the banana leaf does not work. It cannot be sealed, it wilts within hours of cutting, and it provides no protection for liquid items.

The packaging solutions that Kerala meals restaurants have adopted include compartment trays with five or seven sections for the core meal components, typically in PP material. Separate sealed containers for sambar, rasam, and curries, since these liquids must not mix with the rice during transport. Small sauce cups for pickle, which in Kerala means a fiery combination of chillies and oil that will stain anything it touches. Aluminium containers for fish and meat curries, which are oil-heavy and aromatic. Browse our container range for options suited to Kerala meals delivery.

The total packaging cost for a single Kerala meals delivery order typically runs Rs 15-25, depending on the number of components. For a meals restaurant doing 100 delivery orders daily, that is Rs 1,500-2,500 in daily packaging costs. Wholesale purchasing is the only way to keep this manageable.

Eco-Friendly Packaging: Kerala Leads the Way

Kerala is arguably India's most advanced state when it comes to the adoption of eco-friendly food packaging. Several factors drive this. The state's high literacy rate means consumers understand and care about environmental issues. Kerala's tourism industry, particularly in Munnar, Alleppey, Wayanad, and the coastal areas, attracts environmentally conscious travellers who prefer sustainable packaging. The state government has been proactive with regulations, and local bodies like the Kochi Corporation and Trivandrum Corporation have implemented waste management rules that incentivise biodegradable packaging.

Areca Leaf Plates: A Kerala Specialty

Kerala is one of the largest producers of areca leaf plates in India. The areca palm is abundantly grown in the state, and the fallen leaf sheaths are collected, cleaned, heat-pressed into plates and bowls, and sold as a premium eco-friendly alternative to plastic and paper plates. Kerala-made areca leaf plates are exported across India and internationally.

For food businesses within Kerala, areca leaf plates offer a compelling value proposition: they are locally produced (reducing transport costs), culturally appropriate (aligning with Kerala's traditional food presentation on leaves), fully biodegradable, and increasingly affordable as production has scaled up. A basic areca leaf plate costs Rs 2-4, comparable to mid-range paper plates and cheaper than premium bagasse plates.

Bagasse and Paper Alternatives

Bagasse plates and bowls, made from sugarcane fibre, have gained significant traction in Kerala. They handle the oil and moisture content of Kerala food better than paper, they are microwavable, and they are compostable. Kerala's food businesses, particularly the higher-end restaurants and catering services, are increasingly switching to bagasse for takeaway service.

Paper cups for tea and coffee have become the default across Kerala, replacing plastic cups almost entirely in urban areas. The state's tea shop culture, where chai and kattan chaya (black tea) are consumed multiple times daily by virtually everyone, creates enormous demand for small paper cups in the 65-100 ml range. The Malabar Coast: Seafood and Spice Packaging

The Malabar coast from Kozhikode (Calicut) to Kasaragod has a food identity centred on the rich, coconut-based seafood preparations and the Malabar biryani that is distinct from any other biryani in India. The Malabar biryani uses kaima rice instead of basmati, and the preparation method creates a dish that is moister and oilier than its Hyderabadi counterpart. Packaging this biryani requires containers that handle high oil content without leaking and maintain the moisture level without turning the rice soggy.

Calicut's famous Malabar restaurants, the banana leaf joints that serve fish fry, prawn curry, and biryani to packed houses every afternoon, have standardised on aluminium containers for biryani takeaway and PP containers for curries. The fish fry, typically a whole fish marinated and shallow-fried, needs a flat container or wrapping that accommodates its shape without crushing the crispy coating.

The spice trade, centred in Kochi and extending through the entire Western Ghats belt, also generates packaging demand. Spice shops packaging masala powders, whole spices, and spice mixes for retail sale need food-grade pouches with airtight seals to preserve freshness and aroma. While this is more product packaging than food service packaging, many spice shops that also serve food require both types from their suppliers.

Kochi: Kerala's Commercial Packaging Hub

Kochi is the commercial centre of Kerala and the primary wholesale market for food packaging products. The key sourcing areas include Broadway in Ernakulam, which is the traditional wholesale market where packaging traders have operated for decades. Paper cups, plates, bags, and basic containers are available from multiple competing shops. The Edappally-Kakkanad corridor, which has newer distributor warehouses catering to the growing food service sector in the greater Kochi area. And the Kalamassery industrial area, where some local manufacturing of paper cups and packaging products takes place.

Kochi also serves as the distribution hub for packaging products reaching the rest of Kerala. Suppliers in Kochi typically service the entire state, with delivery networks reaching Trivandrum in the south, Calicut in the north, and the hill stations of the Western Ghats. For food businesses outside Kochi, sourcing from Kochi-based distributors or from national wholesale suppliers who ship directly is generally more cost-effective than relying on limited local markets.

Trivandrum and Southern Kerala

Thiruvananthapuram (Trivandrum), as the state capital and a major urban centre, has its own food packaging market driven by government offices, the IT sector in Technopark, and a strong local food culture. Southern Kerala's cuisine is distinct from Malabar in its higher use of coconut and its particular preparation styles for fish and vegetable dishes.

The packaging trends in Trivandrum mirror Kochi's but with a slightly stronger emphasis on eco-friendly options, driven by the environmentally conscious government workforce and the academic community. The Technopark area, with its concentration of IT companies, has a food delivery culture similar to Bangalore's IT corridor, and the packaging demands are comparable: leak-proof containers, branded carry bags, and microwave-safe materials.

The Toddy Shop and Thattu Kada Culture

Kerala's toddy shops (kallu shappu) and thattu kadas (roadside eateries) are institutional-level food establishments that serve millions of meals daily. These are not fine-dining restaurants; they are working-class eating places where a plate of tapioca with fish curry or a kanji (rice gruel) with side dishes is served at prices that the common person can afford. The packaging here is minimal and price-sensitive: paper plates, small PP containers for takeaway curries, and paper or plastic wrapping for parottas.

What makes these establishments significant for the packaging market is sheer volume. Kerala has an estimated 25,000-30,000 thattu kadas and several thousand toddy shops. Even at minimal per-order packaging usage, the aggregate demand is enormous. These businesses are extremely price-sensitive and tend to buy from the cheapest available source, which is often local traders who themselves source from wholesale markets or national suppliers.

Onam and Festival Packaging Demand

Onam, Kerala's harvest festival celebrated in August-September, is the single biggest demand event for food packaging in the state. The Onam sadya, served in homes, restaurants, community halls, and offices across the state, creates a one-week spike in demand for plates (banana leaf for traditional service, compartment trays for packaged delivery), cups for payasam and beverages, containers for the various curries and sides, and carry bags for takeaway orders.

Christmas is another significant demand period, particularly in central Kerala where the Christian population is substantial. The Christmas cake, plum pudding, and special meals drive demand for cake boxes, food containers, and gift packaging. The Thrissur Pooram festival and Vishu (Kerala New Year in April) also create localised demand spikes.

For food businesses and packaging suppliers, Kerala's festival calendar requires planning. Onam packaging should be ordered by early August. Christmas packaging by late November. Wholesale pricing and assured supply depend on advance planning, as the Kerala market's smaller size compared to Maharashtra or UP means that stock availability can be tighter during peak periods.
