Complete Glossary of Food Packaging Terms

March 5, 2025 20 min read How-To Guide

Food packaging has its own language. Whether you are a restaurant owner placing your first wholesale order, a procurement manager evaluating suppliers, or a food entrepreneur building a business plan, understanding packaging terminology saves you from costly misunderstandings. This glossary covers the terms you will encounter when sourcing, specifying, and using food packaging products in India.

A

Antimicrobial Packaging: Packaging that incorporates agents (such as silver ions or natural extracts like neem) that inhibit microbial growth on the food surface. Particularly relevant for fresh meat, dairy, and cut fruit packaging. Read more in our antimicrobial packaging guide.

Aseptic Packaging: A process where the food product and the packaging material are sterilised separately, then combined in a sterile environment. Used for long-shelf-life products like UHT milk, fruit juices, and soups. The Tetra Pak carton is the most recognisable example in India.

Areca Leaf: Packaging material made from the fallen sheaths of the areca palm (supari palm). These naturally moulded plates and bowls require no chemical processing and are fully compostable. Major production centres are in Karnataka and Kerala.

B

Bagasse: The fibrous residue that remains after sugarcane stalks are crushed to extract juice. This agricultural by-product is moulded into plates, bowls, containers, and trays. India produces over 100 million tonnes of bagasse annually, making it one of the most accessible sustainable packaging materials in the country.

Barrier Properties: The ability of packaging material to resist the passage of gases (oxygen, carbon dioxide), moisture, light, and aromas. High-barrier packaging extends shelf life. Aluminium foil has excellent barrier properties; plain paper has poor barrier properties unless coated.

Biodegradable: Material that can be broken down by microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) into natural substances like water, carbon dioxide, and biomass. The timeframe matters -- a material that takes 500 years to biodegrade is technically biodegradable but not practically useful. In the packaging industry, biodegradable typically means breakdown within 1-2 years under natural conditions. See our biodegradable vs compostable comparison.

BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards): The national standards body that sets quality benchmarks for packaging materials sold in India. BIS certification (the ISI mark) on packaging products indicates compliance with Indian safety and quality standards.

Blister Packaging: A pre-formed plastic cavity sealed to a backing card or foil. Common for tablets and capsules in pharma, but also used in food for single-serve portions of butter, jam, honey, and chutney.

C

Caliper: The thickness of paper or board, measured in microns or mils. Higher caliper means thicker, sturdier material. A 250ml paper cup typically uses 170-200 GSM board, while a meal box uses 250-350 GSM.

Clamshell: A hinged, single-piece container that opens and closes like a clam's shell. Eliminates the need for separate lids. Widely used for burgers, sandwiches, and takeaway snacks.

Compostable: Material that breaks down into non-toxic, nutrient-rich compost within a defined timeframe (usually 90-180 days) under specific conditions. Industrial composting requires controlled temperature, moisture, and aeration. Home compostable products break down in garden-level conditions.

Corrugated Board: A layered structure consisting of a fluted (wavy) inner layer sandwiched between two flat linerboard sheets. The fluting creates air pockets that provide cushioning and insulation. Used for pizza boxes, cake boxes, and shipping cartons.

CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board): The government body that enforces plastic waste management rules in India, including the single-use plastic ban. CPCB guidelines determine which packaging products can be legally manufactured and sold.

D

Die-Cutting: A manufacturing process that uses a metal die (shaped blade) to cut packaging material into specific shapes. Used to produce custom-shaped boxes, window cutouts in cake boxes, and intricate packaging designs.

Dona: A traditional Indian leaf bowl made by pressing and stitching sal or banyan leaves. Used at street food stalls, temples, and community meals. The original biodegradable food container.

Duplex Board: A two-layered paperboard with a white top layer and grey back layer. Used for packaging boxes, cartons, and backing cards. Available in various GSM ratings from 230 to 450 GSM.

E

EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility): A regulatory framework that makes producers and brand owners responsible for the end-of-life management of their packaging. Under India's Plastic Waste Management Rules, companies must ensure collection and recycling of packaging waste equivalent to what they generate.

Extrusion: A manufacturing process where plastic material is melted and forced through a die to create continuous shapes -- films, sheets, tubes, and profiles. Most plastic packaging starts as an extruded film or sheet that is then thermoformed into cups, containers, or trays.

F

Film: Thin, flexible packaging material (typically less than 0.25mm thick). Plastic films include PE, PP, PET, and PVC. Paper-based films include cellophane and greaseproof paper. Films are used for wrapping, laminating, and sealing.

FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India): The regulatory body governing food safety in India. All food-contact packaging must comply with FSSAI standards under Regulation 2.4.1, which specifies permissible materials, migration limits, and testing requirements.

Food-Grade: A designation indicating that a material is safe for direct contact with food. Food-grade materials must not transfer harmful chemicals to food above specified migration limits. Always verify food-grade certification when purchasing packaging.

G

Grammage (GSM): Grams per square metre -- the standard measurement for paper and board weight. A 210 GSM paper cup is heavier and sturdier than a 170 GSM cup. Higher GSM generally means better quality and rigidity, but also higher cost.

Greaseproof Paper: Paper that has been treated or manufactured to resist penetration by oils and fats. Achieved through either mechanical treatment (supercalendering to close paper pores) or chemical coating. Essential for wrapping oily Indian snacks like samosas, pakoras, and vada pav.

H

HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene): A strong, chemical-resistant plastic used for milk pouches, carry bags, and rigid containers. Identified by the recycling code 2. HDPE is widely recycled in India and is considered one of the safer food-contact plastics.

Hermetic Seal: An airtight seal that prevents gas exchange between the package interior and the external environment. Critical for products that need protection from oxygen, moisture, or contaminants. Achieved through heat sealing, induction sealing, or adhesive sealing.

I-K

Injection Moulding: A manufacturing process where molten plastic is injected into a mould cavity under pressure. Used to produce rigid packaging items like container lids, tubs, and closures with precise dimensions and thread patterns.

Kraft Paper: Strong, brown paper made from wood pulp using the kraft (sulphate) process. The name comes from the German word for strength. Kraft paper is used extensively for carry bags, meal boxes, wrapping paper, and corrugated board. Its natural brown colour conveys an eco-friendly image.

L

Lamination: The process of bonding two or more layers of material together to combine their properties. A paper cup is laminated with a thin PE layer to make it liquid-proof. Multi-layer laminates combining paper, plastic, and aluminium are used for shelf-stable food pouches.

LDPE (Low-Density Polyethylene): A soft, flexible plastic used for cling films, squeeze bottles, and flexible pouches. Recycling code 4. LDPE has good moisture barrier properties but limited heat resistance.

Leak-proof: Packaging that prevents liquid contents from escaping under normal handling and transport conditions. Achieved through proper sealing, appropriate material selection, and quality manufacturing. Critical for Indian food delivery where gravies and sauces are common.

M

MAP (Modified Atmosphere Packaging): A technique where the atmosphere inside the package is altered (typically by replacing oxygen with nitrogen or carbon dioxide) to slow microbial growth and oxidation. Used for ready-to-eat meals, fresh salads, and bakery products to extend shelf life without preservatives.

Micron: One-thousandth of a millimetre. Used to measure the thickness of plastic films and foils. India's plastic bag regulations specify minimum thickness in microns -- carry bags must now be at least 120 microns thick.

Migration: The transfer of chemical substances from packaging material into food. Regulated by FSSAI and international food safety standards. Migration testing ensures that packaging does not contaminate food with harmful substances above permissible limits.

Microwave-Safe: Packaging that can withstand microwave heating without melting, warping, or releasing harmful chemicals. PP containers, bagasse products, and paper containers are generally microwave-safe. PET, PS, and aluminium are not.

N-O

Nesting: The ability of identical packaging items to stack inside each other to save storage space. Well-designed cups, bowls, and containers nest efficiently, reducing warehouse footprint and transport costs. Poor nesting means you pay to store and ship air.

OTR (Oxygen Transmission Rate): A measure of how much oxygen passes through a packaging material over time. Lower OTR means better oxygen barrier, which extends shelf life for oxygen-sensitive foods. Measured in cc/m2/day.

P

PE (Polyethylene): The most widely used plastic family, including HDPE, LDPE, and LLDPE. PE is used for films, bags, bottle caps, and as a coating on paper cups and cartons. Generally considered food-safe and is recyclable.

PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate): A clear, strong, lightweight plastic used for water bottles, transparent containers, and blister packaging. Recycling code 1. PET is the most recycled plastic in India. Food-grade PET is safe for single use but should not be repeatedly reused or heated.

PLA (Polylactic Acid): A bioplastic derived from corn starch or sugarcane. PLA looks and feels like conventional plastic but is compostable under industrial conditions. Used for cold cups, salad containers, and transparent lids. Limited heat resistance (softens above 50 degrees Celsius) restricts its use for hot foods.

PP (Polypropylene): A versatile, heat-resistant plastic used for microwave-safe containers, yoghurt cups, and hot-fill applications. Recycling code 5. PP withstands temperatures up to 130 degrees Celsius, making it the preferred plastic for reheatable food containers.

PS (Polystyrene): Available in two forms -- rigid (crystal PS for cups and containers) and expanded (EPS/thermocol for insulating trays and cups). Expanded polystyrene is banned for food service in most Indian states under the single-use plastic rules.

Q-R

QSR (Quick Service Restaurant): Industry term for fast-food restaurants. QSR packaging requirements emphasise speed of packing, portion standardisation, and brand visibility. QSR chains are among the largest consumers of disposable food packaging in India.

Recyclable: Material that can be collected, processed, and converted into new products. In India, the practical recyclability of a material depends on local collection infrastructure and rag-picker networks. Aluminium, PET, and HDPE have well-established recycling chains; multi-layer laminates are difficult to recycle.

Retort Packaging: Flexible pouches or rigid containers that can withstand high-temperature sterilisation (typically 121 degrees Celsius for 20-40 minutes). Used for ready-to-eat meals like rajma, dal makhani, and paneer tikka masala that can be stored at room temperature for months.

S

Shelf Life: The period during which a packaged food product remains safe, retains its quality, and meets labelling claims. Packaging choice directly affects shelf life through its barrier properties, seal integrity, and material interactions.

Shrink Film: A polymer film that shrinks when heat is applied, conforming tightly to the product shape. Used for bundling bottles, wrapping trays, and tamper-evident seals. Available in PVC, PE, and POF (polyolefin) materials.

Single-Use Plastic: Plastic products designed to be used once and then discarded. India has banned 19 categories of single-use plastics since July 2022, including plates, cups, straws, cutlery, and thin carry bags.

Snap-Fit Lid: A lid that clicks into place on a container rim through a mechanical interlock. Provides a secure seal without the need for heat sealing or adhesives. The most common closure mechanism for takeaway food containers.

T

Tamper-Evident: Packaging features that visibly indicate whether the package has been opened or interfered with. Examples include shrink bands, breakable seals, and adhesive labels that tear when removed. Increasingly required by food delivery platforms in India.

Thermoforming: A manufacturing process where a plastic sheet is heated and shaped over a mould using vacuum or pressure. Used to produce cups, trays, containers, and blister packs. Thermoforming is the primary manufacturing method for disposable food containers.

U-Z

UV Protection: Packaging features that block ultraviolet light, which can degrade food colour, flavour, and nutritional value. Amber or opaque packaging provides natural UV protection. UV-blocking additives can be incorporated into transparent packaging.

Vacuum Packaging: Removing air from a package before sealing to extend shelf life by eliminating oxygen. Used for paneer, cheese, meat, and ready-to-eat products. Requires a vacuum sealing machine and multi-layer barrier pouches.

WVTR (Water Vapour Transmission Rate): A measure of how much water vapour passes through a packaging material over time. Important for products that must stay dry (crackers, spices) or moist (bread, cakes). Measured in g/m2/day.
