Holi Food Packaging Guide for Restaurants and Caterers

February 20, 2025 12 min read Food Packaging

Holi is India's most exuberant festival, and the food that accompanies it is just as vibrant as the colours. From creamy thandai laced with almonds and saffron to crispy gujiya stuffed with khoya, from mountains of namkeen to platters of dahi bhalle -- Holi food is rich, varied, and deeply tied to regional traditions. For food businesses, this means a surge in orders that requires packaging capable of handling everything from liquid beverages to oil-rich sweets to crispy snacks, often in outdoor settings where colour, water, and general festive chaos are part of the environment.

If you run a restaurant, sweet shop, catering business, or cloud kitchen, Holi represents a concentrated burst of demand that can be highly profitable -- provided your packaging is up to the task. This guide walks you through the specific packaging challenges of Holi and how to address each one.

The Unique Challenges of Holi Food Packaging

Holi is unlike most other Indian festivals when it comes to food packaging demands. Here is why:

Colour contamination: Holi colours -- whether dry gulal or wet colours -- get everywhere. Hands covered in colour will handle your packaging. If the packaging is porous, colour particles can seep through and contaminate food. This is not just an aesthetic problem; many Holi colours contain chemicals that are not food-safe.

Water exposure: Water balloons, pichkaris, and buckets of coloured water are the norm. Any packaging that cannot withstand moisture is going to fail. Paper bags, uncoated cardboard boxes, and thin paper plates will turn into soggy mush within minutes of exposure.

Outdoor serving: Most Holi celebrations happen outdoors -- in courtyards, terraces, parks, and streets. This means packaging needs to withstand direct sunlight, wind, and the general rough handling that comes with outdoor festivities.

Greasy and liquid foods: Holi menus are heavy on fried items (gujiya, mathri, papdi) and liquid preparations (thandai, lassi, kanji). Packaging needs to handle both grease and liquids without leaking or becoming structurally compromised.

Packaging Solutions by Food Category

Thandai and Beverages

Thandai is the signature Holi beverage, and demand spikes dramatically in the days leading up to and on the festival day. For businesses serving thandai in volume, paper cups with PE coating are the most practical option. The coating prevents the liquid from soaking through and provides a 30-45 minute window of structural integrity, which is more than enough for event consumption.

For premium thandai service -- such as at hotel Holi brunches or corporate events -- consider double-wall paper cups that offer a better grip and a more upscale look. Pair them with dome lids if the thandai has garnishes like chopped almonds and rose petals that tend to spill during outdoor celebrations.

Quantity estimate: For a Holi event of 200 guests, plan for 400-500 cups (most guests will have 2-3 servings of thandai and other beverages).

Gujiya and Fried Sweets

Gujiya is to Holi what ladoo is to Diwali. These crescent-shaped stuffed pastries are fried and often coated in sugar syrup, making them both greasy and sticky. For retail packaging, food-grade boxes with grease-resistant lining work well. Place butter paper between layers to prevent sticking.

For event serving, use sturdy disposable plates -- ideally compartment plates that keep gujiya separate from drier items like mathri or namkeen. Avoid thin paper plates that will buckle under the weight of oil-rich sweets.

Namkeen and Dry Snacks

Namkeen (sev, mixture, papdi, chakli) is a Holi staple served in enormous quantities. The primary packaging concern is maintaining crispness. Use containers with tight-fitting lids for pre-packed portions, or serve from large containers into individual bowls. If packaging namkeen for gifting, ensure the container is airtight -- Holi falls in March, and the pre-monsoon humidity in many parts of India is already high enough to make namkeen soggy within hours of exposure.

Chaat and Wet Snacks

Dahi bhalle, papdi chaat, and ragda pattice are popular Holi foods that involve yoghurt, chutneys, and sev. These are messy by nature and need leak-proof bowls with sufficient depth to contain the liquids. Pair with disposable spoons -- forks are impractical for chaat, and eating with colour-stained hands is not something most people prefer.

Quantity Planning for Holi Events

Whether you are catering a residential society Holi party or managing a restaurant's Holi special menu, here is a practical guide for quantity planning:

Residential Society Party (100-200 people)

  • Paper cups (for thandai/lassi): 400-600
  • Disposable plates: 200-300
  • Small bowls (for chaat): 200-300
  • Spoons: 300-400
  • Napkins/tissue paper: 500-800
  • Food-grade aluminium trays (for serving): 10-15

Restaurant Holi Special (Single Day)

  • Takeaway containers: 150-300
  • Beverage cups with lids: 200-400
  • Sweet boxes (gujiya packs): 100-200
  • Carry bags: 150-300
  • Napkins: 500+

Large Holi Event (500+ people)

  • Paper cups: 1,500-2,000
  • Plates: 600-800
  • Bowls: 600-800
  • Spoons and forks: 800-1,000
  • Aluminium foil serving trays: 30-50
  • Tissue paper and napkins: 2,000+

Packaging Tips Specific to Holi

Use colour-resistant outer packaging: White and light-coloured packaging shows colour stains immediately. Consider using packaging in darker tones or with printed designs that camouflage minor colour marks. This keeps the presentation clean even in a messy Holi environment.

Pre-pack everything possible: During Holi celebrations, the pace of serving is fast and the environment is chaotic. Pre-pack individual portions of gujiya, namkeen, and sweets so that distribution is quick and hygienic. People with colour-stained hands should not be digging into shared containers.

Double-bag liquid items: If delivering thandai or other beverages, place the sealed cups inside a secondary bag. Holi deliveries encounter more rough handling than usual, and a spilled thandai delivery is a complete loss -- both the product and the customer's trust.

Seal everything: Open containers and uncovered plates are invitations for colour powder to settle on food. Use containers with lids, seal boxes with tape, and cover serving trays with cling film or food wrapping until the moment of serving.

Holi Gifting Packages

Holi gifting is smaller in scale than Diwali but growing steadily, especially in corporate circles. A typical Holi gift box might include gujiya, namkeen, thandai powder/concentrate, and dry fruits. For these hampers, use rigid or semi-rigid boxes with compartment inserts to keep items separated.

The colour palette for Holi gifting boxes traditionally leans toward bright, warm tones -- saffron, magenta, green, and gold. This is one festival where vibrant packaging design genuinely matches the cultural mood, so do not hold back on colour if you are getting custom boxes printed.

Post-Holi Cleanup: The Packaging Angle

Responsible businesses think about what happens to their packaging after the celebration. Holi generates significant waste, and packaging that is easy to collect, stack, and dispose of (or compost) reflects well on your brand. Bagasse plates and cups are compostable. Aluminium containers are recyclable. Encourage customers to separate waste by providing clearly marked bins at events you cater.
