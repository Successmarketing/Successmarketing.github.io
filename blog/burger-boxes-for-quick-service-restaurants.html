Burger Boxes for Quick Service Restaurants: Complete Guide

June 18, 2025 11 min read Boxes

India's burger market has grown far beyond the international chains. From gourmet burger joints in metro cities to street-side vada pav stalls adding Western-style burgers to their menus, the demand for proper burger packaging has never been higher. Cloud kitchens focused entirely on burgers are among the fastest-growing food delivery segments, and every one of them needs the right box.

A burger is a structurally delicate food item. It has layers that can shift, sauces that can leak, and a bun that can turn from pleasantly soft to unpleasantly soggy in minutes if packaged poorly. The right burger box protects this structure while keeping the burger warm and presentable. This guide helps you choose wisely.

Why Burger Packaging Deserves Special Attention

Unlike a plate of rice and curry that sits relatively stable in a container, a burger presents unique packaging challenges:

  • Height and structure: A stacked burger with patty, lettuce, tomato, cheese, and sauces can be 8-10 cm tall. The box needs to accommodate this height without compressing the burger.
  • Moisture from multiple sources: Steam from the hot patty, moisture from fresh vegetables, and condensation from sauces all create a humid environment inside the box that can soften the bun.
  • Grease management: Burger patties release significant grease, especially when hot. The box needs to handle this without becoming structurally weak or leaking.
  • Lateral movement: During delivery, a burger can slide inside the box, causing toppings to shift. The box should fit snugly enough to limit movement but not so tight that it squashes the burger.

Types of Burger Boxes

Clamshell Burger Boxes

The most popular format for burger packaging, clamshell boxes feature a hinged lid that opens to reveal the burger and snaps shut for transport. They are fast to use (open, place burger, close) and the snap closure provides security during delivery.

Clamshell burger boxes come in foam, PP, and bagasse materials. For quick-service restaurants handling high volumes, PP clamshells offer the best balance of speed, durability, and cost. Bagasse clamshells are increasingly popular with premium burger brands that want an eco-friendly image.

Corrugated Cardboard Burger Boxes

Corrugated boxes provide superior insulation and a premium feel. They are commonly used by gourmet burger restaurants and brands that want their packaging to make a statement. The rigid structure protects the burger well during transport, and the cardboard surface is ideal for full-colour branding.

The main drawback is speed — cardboard boxes that need to be folded and assembled take longer to prepare than clamshells. Many restaurants pre-fold boxes during off-peak hours to have them ready for rush periods.

Paper Wraps with Box

Some burger businesses wrap the burger in grease-proof paper first, then place it in a simple open-top box or paper bag. This hybrid approach offers excellent grease management (the paper absorbs initial grease), moderate structural protection, and a casual, street-food aesthetic that works well for certain brand identities.

This method is particularly popular in India for vada pav-style burgers and fusion burgers sold from food trucks and small takeaway counters.

Kraft Paper Boxes

Simple kraft paper boxes with a tuck-in lid are an economical option for basic burger packaging. They provide adequate protection for counter pickup and short-distance delivery. The natural brown finish gives a wholesome, artisanal look that resonates with health-food and organic burger brands.

Choosing the Right Size

Burger boxes need to match your actual burger dimensions, which vary more than you might expect across different menus. Here is a sizing guide:

Burger Type Typical Dimensions Recommended Box Size
Small / Slider 6-7 cm diameter, 5-6 cm tall Small clamshell (10 x 10 x 7 cm)
Regular Burger 9-10 cm diameter, 7-8 cm tall Medium clamshell (12 x 12 x 8 cm)
Premium / Gourmet 10-12 cm diameter, 9-12 cm tall Large clamshell (15 x 15 x 10 cm)
Double / Stacked 10 cm diameter, 12-15 cm tall Deep box (15 x 15 x 12 cm)
Burger + Fries Combo Variable Compartment box or separate containers

The most important measurement is the height. A box that is too short compresses the burger, pushing toppings out and flattening the bun. Always measure the tallest burger on your menu and ensure your box accommodates it with at least 1 cm of clearance.

Material Comparison for Indian Conditions

Indian weather and food preferences create specific demands on burger box materials. Here is how each material performs under real-world Indian conditions:

PP (Polypropylene)

Handles India's heat and humidity well. PP does not absorb moisture, so the box maintains structural integrity even during monsoon season when ambient humidity is high. It manages grease effectively — the material is naturally grease-resistant, so even a juicy double-patty burger will not compromise the box.

PP is microwave-safe, which is a plus for customers who want to reheat a delivered burger. It is also the most cost-effective option for high-volume operations.

Bagasse (Sugarcane Fibre)

Bagasse burger boxes have gained strong traction in the Indian market. They absorb some grease (which prevents pooling), are microwave-safe, and have a natural aesthetic that photographs well — important in the age of Instagram food shots. They are compostable, aligning with growing environmental awareness.

However, bagasse can soften if exposed to excessive moisture or steam for extended periods. For burgers that are very saucy or that will sit in the box for more than 30-40 minutes, PP may be more reliable.

Corrugated Cardboard

Provides the best insulation and the most premium feel. However, cardboard is vulnerable to grease saturation, so burger boxes should have a food-grade grease-resistant coating or lining on the interior. Without this lining, a greasy burger can weaken the cardboard within 15-20 minutes.

Ventilation vs. Insulation: The Burger Dilemma

Burgers present a packaging paradox: you want to keep them warm (requiring insulation), but you also want to prevent the bun from getting soggy (requiring ventilation). The best burger packaging balances both needs.

Strategies that work:

  • Micro-perforated boxes: Small holes that are large enough to release steam but small enough to retain most heat. These are available in both PP and bagasse formats.
  • Paper liner inside the box: A sheet of grease-proof paper between the burger and the box absorbs moisture and grease. This is one of the simplest and most effective improvements you can make to your burger packaging.
  • Bottom ventilation: Some premium burger boxes have raised ridges or perforations on the bottom that allow steam and grease to move away from the burger. This prevents the bottom bun from sitting in a pool of condensation.
  • Timing: The best packaging in the world cannot overcome physics. Package burgers as close to pickup or delivery handoff as possible. A burger packaged at the right moment will always outperform one that has been sitting in a box waiting.

Packaging for Burger Combos

Most QSR orders include a burger with fries, a drink, and perhaps a dip or side. Packaging the complete combo requires thought about how items interact:

Option 1: Separate Packaging

Burger in its own box, fries in a paper bag or sleeve, drink in a cup with lid. All items placed in a carrier bag. This gives each item its own optimised packaging but requires more materials and assembly time.

Option 2: Combo Box

A larger box with sections for the burger and fries, sometimes with a drink holder built in. These combo boxes reduce overall packaging count and present a more unified meal experience. They are popular with delivery-focused brands because they simplify the delivery person's job.

Option 3: Burger Box + Fry Cone/Pouch

A middle ground where the burger gets its own box and the fries go into a cone or paper pouch that fits alongside or inside the main box. This keeps the fries' heat and grease separate from the burger while keeping the overall package compact.

Branding Opportunities with Burger Boxes

Burger boxes are prime branding real estate. The box is the first thing a customer sees when their delivery arrives, and it is often photographed and shared on social media. Here is how to maximise the branding opportunity:

  • Top of box: Your logo, large and clear. This is the money shot for Instagram and food delivery platform photos.
  • Inside of lid: A fun message, a coupon code for the next order, or your social media handles. Customers see this as they open the box — it is a moment of anticipation you can leverage.
  • Side panels: Your website, phone number, and a brief brand story. Keep it concise.
  • Bottom: Nutritional information, allergen warnings, and recycling instructions if applicable.

Best Practices for Burger Packing

  1. Keep assembly tight: Push the burger gently into the box so it fits snugly without excessive space for sliding.
  2. Wrap wet burgers: If your burger has significant sauce or moisture, wrap it in grease-proof paper before boxing. This adds a few seconds per burger but dramatically improves the delivered product.
  3. Pack fries separately: Fries release steam that softens burger buns. Always keep them in a separate container or section.
  4. Close the box fully: A partially closed clamshell lets heat escape rapidly. Ensure every box is clicked shut or sealed before handing to the delivery person.
  5. Stack flat during delivery: Instruct delivery partners to keep burger boxes horizontal. A tilted box causes the burger to slide and compress against one side.
