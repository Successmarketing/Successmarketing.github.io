Organic Food Packaging Requirements in India: What Every Business Must Know

January 18, 2025 15 min read Food Packaging

India's organic food market has crossed Rs 12,000 crore and is projected to grow at over 20% annually through the end of this decade. What was once a niche segment confined to weekend farmers' markets in metros has spread to Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities through online grocery platforms and dedicated organic stores. Brands like Organic Tattva, 24 Mantra, and dozens of regional players have made organic staples, snacks, and ready-to-eat meals accessible to a broad consumer base that is willing to pay a premium for chemical-free, sustainably grown food.

But here is where many organic food businesses stumble: they invest heavily in sourcing certified organic ingredients, follow organic processing standards meticulously, and then pack the finished product in packaging that either violates organic certification norms, leaches chemicals into the food, or simply fails to communicate the organic promise to the end consumer. Packaging is the final link between your organic product and the customer's trust, and getting it wrong can undermine everything that came before.

This guide walks through the regulatory, material, and practical requirements for packaging organic food products in India, whether you are a packaged organic food brand, an organic restaurant, or a farm-to-fork delivery service.

FSSAI Organic Food Regulations and Packaging

The Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) governs organic food through the Food Safety and Standards (Organic Foods) Regulations, 2017. These regulations have specific implications for packaging that every organic food business must understand.

Certification requirement: Any food product sold as "organic" in India must be certified under either the National Programme for Organic Production (NPOP) managed by the Ministry of Commerce, or the Participatory Guarantee System (PGS-India) managed by the Ministry of Agriculture. The packaging must display the appropriate certification mark. For NPOP-certified products, this is the India Organic logo. For PGS-India certified products, the Jaivik Bharat logo must appear on the packaging.

Labelling requirements: Beyond the certification logo, the FSSAI mandates that organic food packaging must include the name and address of the certifying body, the certification or license number, and the FSSAI logo with license number. The word "organic" can only appear on labels of products that have valid organic certification. Using the word "organic" on packaging without valid certification is a punishable offence under FSSAI regulations.

Traceability: Organic food packaging should enable traceability back to the source. This typically means including batch numbers, date of packing, and ideally, the name of the organic farm or producer group. Some brands use QR codes on packaging that link to traceability information, which builds consumer confidence and satisfies regulatory expectations.

Packaging Materials That Align with Organic Values

The choice of packaging material matters enormously for organic food, both for regulatory compliance and consumer perception. Organic customers are discerning. They read labels, scrutinise materials, and will question the integrity of an organic brand that uses excessive plastic or non-recyclable packaging.

Kraft Paper and Cardboard

Kraft paper is the workhorse material for organic food packaging in India. Its natural brown colour, earthy texture, and recyclability align perfectly with the organic brand identity. Kraft paper bags, boxes, and pouches are widely used for organic staples like rice, pulses, flour, spices, and snacks.

For moisture-sensitive organic products, kraft paper with an inner PE or PLA lining provides a moisture barrier while maintaining the natural exterior appearance. Our kraft paper box range is popular with organic food brands for exactly this reason. Unlined kraft paper works well for dry products like organic muesli, roasted snacks, and whole spices.

Glass and Metal

Glass jars remain the gold standard for organic preserves, honey, pickles, ghee, and cold-pressed oils. Glass is inert, meaning it does not react with or leach into the food, making it the safest material for organic products that sit on shelves for extended periods. The main drawback is weight and breakage risk, which increases shipping costs.

Tin-plated steel and food-grade aluminium containers are used for organic cooking oils and some processed foods. These materials offer excellent barrier properties against light, moisture, and oxygen.

Bagasse and Plant-Based Containers

For organic restaurants and meal delivery services, bagasse containers made from sugarcane fibre provide an excellent sustainable option. They are compostable, microwave-safe, and visually communicate the natural, earth-friendly values that organic customers expect. Areca palm leaf plates are another option that resonates strongly with the organic positioning.

Food-Grade Plastic: When and How

Let us address the elephant in the room. Many organic food brands use plastic packaging, particularly for products that require strong moisture and oxygen barriers, like organic snacks, ready-to-eat meals, and frozen items. The organic regulations do not prohibit plastic packaging, but they do require that all food-contact packaging materials comply with FSSAI's packaging standards under IS 10146 (for polyethylene) and IS 10142 (for styrene polymers).

If you use plastic, make it food-grade and ensure it is free from recycled content (recycled plastic can carry contaminants from its previous life). Polypropylene (PP) and high-density polyethylene (HDPE) are the safest plastic options for food contact. Avoid PVC and polystyrene for organic food packaging, as both have higher leaching risks and are difficult to recycle.

Migration and Chemical Safety Standards

Organic consumers are particularly concerned about chemical migration from packaging into food. This concern is valid. All food packaging materials can potentially transfer small amounts of substances into the food they contain. For organic food, where the entire value proposition rests on being free from synthetic chemicals, packaging-related contamination is a serious brand risk.

Indian Standards for migration: The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) sets limits on the migration of substances from packaging into food. IS 9845 covers overall migration limits for plastics in food contact. The overall migration limit is 60 mg/kg of food (or 10 mg/dm2 of packaging surface area). Specific migration limits exist for individual substances like lead, cadmium, and various plasticisers.

Ink and printing safety: If you print on organic food packaging (logos, labels, nutritional information), the printing inks must not migrate through the packaging into the food. Use food-safe inks, and ensure that printed surfaces do not come into direct contact with the food. For kraft paper packaging, printing on the outer surface with a food-contact-safe inner layer is the standard approach.

Adhesives and seals: The adhesives used in packaging construction (gluing box flaps, sealing pouches) must also be food-safe. Hot-melt adhesives and water-based adhesives are generally safer than solvent-based adhesives for organic food packaging.

Packaging for Specific Organic Product Categories

Organic Grains, Pulses, and Flour

These staple products are the bread and butter of India's organic food market. They need packaging that protects against moisture, insects, and contamination while allowing the consumer to see or feel the product quality.

Stand-up pouches with a kraft paper exterior and PE inner lining are the most common format. Sizes range from 500 grams to 5 kilograms. Include a resealable zip-lock closure for consumer convenience. The packaging must display the net weight, MRP, FSSAI license number, organic certification mark, and best-before date. For bulk or wholesale packaging, woven PP bags with an inner PE liner are standard, with the organic certification printed directly on the bag.

Organic Spices and Condiments

Spices are volatile products that lose aroma and potency when exposed to light, air, or moisture. Organic spices need packaging with excellent barrier properties. Aluminium-lined pouches or small glass jars with airtight seals work best. For ground spices, nitrogen flushing inside the pouch displaces oxygen and extends shelf life significantly.

A common mistake among small organic spice brands is using clear packaging to show the product colour. While this helps visual appeal, it exposes the spice to light degradation. A better approach is a mostly opaque package with a small clear window that lets the consumer see the product without exposing the entire contents to light.

Organic Ready-to-Eat and Frozen Foods

The organic ready-to-eat segment is growing rapidly in Indian metros. Products include organic dal makhani, rajma, chole, soups, and frozen organic parathas and snacks. These products need packaging that can withstand either retort processing (for shelf-stable RTE meals) or freezing temperatures.

Retort pouches (multi-layer laminated pouches that can be sterilised) are the standard for shelf-stable organic meals. For frozen products, packaging must maintain integrity at -18 degrees Celsius without cracking or delaminating. Both applications typically require plastic-based packaging, but choosing recyclable mono-material structures over non-recyclable multi-layer laminates is a meaningful step toward sustainability.

Organic Beverages and Dairy Alternatives

Organic juices, almond milk, coconut water, and herbal teas each have specific packaging needs. Tetra Pak-style cartons dominate the organic beverage space for shelf-stable products. For fresh organic juices sold through refrigerated channels, PET bottles or glass bottles are common. Paper cups with PLA lining work well for organic tea and coffee served at cafes and events.

Organic Restaurant and Cloud Kitchen Packaging

The organic restaurant segment in India has its own packaging challenges, distinct from packaged organic food brands. When you run an organic restaurant or cloud kitchen, every touchpoint, from the container to the cutlery to the carry bag, needs to feel consistent with the organic promise.

Container selection: Use bagasse or kraft-based food containers as your default. Reserve plastic containers only for items with high liquid content where leak-proof sealing is non-negotiable. Clearly label each container with an "organic" sticker or stamp.

Cutlery: Wooden or bamboo cutlery is the natural choice. Avoid plastic cutlery entirely if your brand identity centres on organic and natural values. The cost difference is Rs 2-4 per set, which is negligible relative to the average organic meal price point.

Carry bags: Paper or cloth carry bags with your organic branding. Single-use plastic carry bags are banned in most Indian states anyway, and even where technically permitted, they contradict the organic positioning.

Napkins and wipes: Unbleached, recycled-content paper napkins are a small detail that reinforces the organic message. Avoid bright white napkins, which are typically chlorine-bleached.

Common Packaging Mistakes Organic Businesses Make

Using "organic" without certification: This is the most serious mistake and carries legal consequences. If your product is not certified organic under NPOP or PGS-India, you cannot use the word "organic" on your packaging. Terms like "natural," "farm-fresh," or "chemical-free" are not regulated the same way and can be used more freely, though they carry their own consumer expectations.

Overpackaging: Many organic brands use excessive packaging layers, sometimes wrapping a glass jar in a cardboard box with tissue paper and a paper bag. While premium presentation matters, organic consumers are more likely to be irritated by waste than impressed by layers of packaging. Use minimal, purposeful packaging.

Ignoring shelf life: Organic food products, particularly those without synthetic preservatives, often have shorter shelf lives. Your packaging must be designed to maximise freshness within this shorter window. Poor barrier packaging that allows moisture or oxygen ingress can lead to spoilage before the stated best-before date, which is both a food safety and brand trust issue.

Inconsistent branding: Some organic businesses invest in beautiful primary packaging (the product container) but use generic, unbranded secondary packaging (the shipping box or carry bag). Every packaging touchpoint should reinforce the organic identity consistently.

Organic food already commands a premium over conventional food, typically 20-50% more, so adding expensive packaging can squeeze margins further. Here is a realistic cost framework:
