Organic Food Packaging Requirements in India: Complete Guide

January 18, 2025 15 min read Food Packaging

India's organic food market crossed Rs 12,000 crore in 2024 and continues to grow at roughly 20% year-on-year. What was once limited to a few niche stores in metro cities has become a mainstream grocery category, with organic sections in every major supermarket chain and dedicated organic brands flourishing on Amazon, BigBasket, and local delivery platforms. Sikkim became India's first fully organic state in 2016, and states like Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, and Uttarakhand have rapidly expanded organic farming acreage under the Paramparagat Krishi Vikas Yojana and National Programme for Organic Production.

But here is the problem many organic food businesses run into: they invest heavily in sourcing certified organic ingredients, maintaining clean supply chains, and building trust with health-conscious consumers, only to package their products in materials that either compromise the organic integrity or fail to meet regulatory requirements. Packaging is the last link in the chain before the product reaches the consumer, and getting it wrong can undermine everything that came before it.

This guide covers the regulatory framework, material choices, labelling requirements, and practical packaging decisions that organic food businesses in India need to get right.

What "Organic" Actually Means for Packaging

Before we get into specifics, it helps to clarify a common confusion. When we say "organic food packaging," we are not necessarily talking about packaging made from organic materials. We are talking about packaging that is suitable for organic food products, meaning it preserves the organic integrity of the food inside, meets FSSAI and certification body requirements, and does not introduce contaminants that would compromise the product's organic status.

Of course, many organic food brands also prefer eco-friendly packaging materials for brand alignment, and we will cover those options. But the regulatory requirement is about protecting what is inside the package, not about the package material being organic itself.

FSSAI Regulations for Organic Food Packaging

The Food Safety and Standards Authority of India governs organic food through the Food Safety and Standards (Organic Foods) Regulations, 2017, along with subsequent amendments. Here are the packaging-specific requirements:

Jaivik Bharat Logo

All organic food products sold in India must carry the Jaivik Bharat logo, which is the official mark of organic certification. The logo must be clearly visible on the primary packaging and must meet specific size and colour requirements. It cannot be smaller than the brand logo and must be printed in the designated green colour on a white background. The logo indicates that the product has been certified under either the National Programme for Organic Production (NPOP) or the Participatory Guarantee System (PGS-India).

FSSAI Licence Number

The 14-digit FSSAI licence number must be displayed on the packaging along with the FSSAI logo. For organic food specifically, FSSAI also requires the name of the certification body and the certification number to be printed on the label.

Labelling Requirements

Organic food packaging must include the following label information, in addition to the standard FSSAI labelling requirements for all food products:

  • The word "Organic" or "Certified Organic" or "Certified Organic by [certification body name]"
  • Name and address of the certification body
  • Certification/licence number
  • Jaivik Bharat logo
  • FSSAI logo and licence number
  • Net quantity, batch number, date of manufacture, and best before date
  • List of ingredients (with organic ingredients clearly identified)
  • Nutritional information per 100g or per serving
  • Name and address of the manufacturer/packer

Products that contain less than 95% organic ingredients cannot be labelled as "organic" but may state "Made with organic [ingredient name]" if at least 70% of the ingredients are organic.

Material Contact Requirements

FSSAI's Food Safety and Standards (Packaging) Regulations, 2018 apply to all food packaging, including organic products. The key provision is that packaging material must not transfer harmful substances to food above the prescribed migration limits. This includes overall migration limits for plastics (60 mg/kg) and specific migration limits for individual substances like heavy metals, plasticizers, and monomers.

For organic food, many certification bodies impose stricter requirements, prohibiting packaging materials that contain BPA, phthalates, or other endocrine-disrupting chemicals. Check with your specific certification body (APEDA-accredited agencies for NPOP, or PGS groups) for their packaging material guidelines.

Packaging Materials for Organic Food Products

Glass Containers

Glass remains the gold standard for organic food packaging, particularly for products like organic honey, ghee, pickles, oils, and jams. It is chemically inert, meaning it does not react with food or transfer any substances. Glass is impermeable to gases and moisture, providing excellent shelf life. It is also infinitely recyclable without quality degradation.

The drawbacks are weight (increasing transport costs), fragility (higher breakage during logistics), and cost (significantly more expensive than plastic). For online sales, glass packaging requires additional protective padding, adding to packaging costs. Despite these disadvantages, glass communicates quality and purity to consumers, which is why premium organic brands overwhelmingly choose it for retail products.

Food-Grade Paper and Kraft Packaging

Paper and kraft-based packaging works well for dry organic products: cereals, flours, spices, tea, dried fruits, and snacks. Kraft paper boxes and bags align with the natural, unprocessed image of organic food. Use food-grade, unbleached paper where possible, as bleached paper may contain chlorine residues that some organic certification bodies find objectionable.

For moisture-sensitive products like organic spices or tea, paper packaging alone is insufficient. You need either a sealed inner liner (food-grade PE or foil) or a laminated paper structure. The inner layer protects against moisture and oxygen, while the outer kraft layer provides the branding surface and structural integrity.

Food-Grade Plastics

Despite the preference for natural materials, food-grade plastics remain practical and often necessary for certain organic products. PP (polypropylene, code 5) and HDPE (high-density polyethylene, code 2) are the most commonly used and generally accepted for organic food. They are BPA-free, chemically stable, and cost-effective.

Avoid PVC (code 3) and polystyrene (code 6) for organic food packaging. PVC contains plasticizers including phthalates, and polystyrene can leach styrene, a suspected carcinogen. Most organic certification bodies prohibit these materials for direct food contact.

Compostable and Biodegradable Options

Many organic brands choose compostable packaging to align with their sustainability messaging. Bagasse containers, areca leaf plates, and PLA-lined paper cups are popular choices for organic food service. While not a regulatory requirement, using sustainable packaging reinforces the organic brand promise and resonates with the target consumer.

However, compostable packaging has practical limitations. It typically offers shorter shelf life for packaged products compared to conventional plastics, and moisture resistance is lower. For retail organic products that sit on shelves for weeks or months, conventional food-grade packaging with proper barrier properties usually outperforms compostable alternatives.

Packaging for Specific Organic Product Categories

Organic Staples: Rice, Dal, Flour, and Grains

These high-volume products need packaging that protects against moisture, pests, and oxidation over a shelf life of 3-12 months. Multi-layer laminated pouches (BOPP/PE or PET/PE) are the industry standard. The outer layer provides printability and puncture resistance, while the inner PE layer provides heat-sealability and moisture barrier.

For premium positioning, many organic brands use kraft paper stand-up pouches with a PE or foil inner layer. These combine the natural aesthetic of paper with the functional protection of a barrier layer. The zipper reseal is important for products that are consumed over multiple uses.

Organic Spices and Masalas

Spices are extremely sensitive to moisture, light, and oxygen. Exposure to any of these degrades flavour, colour, and aroma rapidly. Use laminated pouches with aluminium foil as the barrier layer for maximum protection, or metalized BOPP for a lighter-weight alternative. Opaque or semi-opaque packaging that blocks light is essential.

Small spice quantities (50-100g) for retail can use rigid PET or glass jars with airtight lids. Larger wholesale quantities (500g-1kg) typically use flexible pouches for cost efficiency.

Organic Oils and Ghee

Glass bottles are strongly preferred for organic cold-pressed oils and ghee. The visual clarity of glass allows consumers to see the product, and the inert nature of glass ensures no flavour transfer. Dark glass (amber or green) is ideal for oils that are sensitive to light degradation, such as flaxseed oil or extra virgin olive oil.

PET bottles are a lighter, cheaper alternative but allow some oxygen permeation over time, which can reduce shelf life. For organic ghee, wide-mouth glass jars are the market standard because ghee solidifies at room temperature and needs to be scooped out.

Organic Ready-to-Eat and Meal Delivery

The organic meal delivery segment is growing rapidly in Indian metros. Companies delivering organic tiffins, meal plans, and health bowls need packaging that is microwave-safe, leak-proof, and ideally eco-friendly. Compartment containers in 750-1000 ml sizes are the standard format, allowing rice, curry, and sides to be packed without mixing.

Bagasse containers work well for same-day delivery organic meals where extended shelf life is not a concern. For multi-day meal plans that include refrigerated storage, PP containers with tight-fitting lids are more practical.

Common Mistakes in Organic Food Packaging

Having worked with organic food businesses across Rajasthan and beyond for over three decades, we see certain mistakes repeatedly:

Using recycled packaging for direct food contact: Recycled paper and cardboard can contain contaminants from the recycling process, including mineral oils from printing inks, which migrate into food. For direct food contact, use virgin paper or ensure recycled material meets FSSAI migration limits and has a food-grade inner barrier.

Ignoring the certification body's packaging guidelines: FSSAI sets the minimum requirements, but your specific organic certification body may have additional rules about permitted materials, printing inks, and adhesives. Failing to follow these can jeopardise your organic certification during audits.

Oversizing packaging: Using packaging that is much larger than the product creates the impression of less product and more waste, both of which alienate organic food consumers. Right-size your packaging to fit the product with minimal empty space.

Neglecting tamper evidence: Organic food commands a premium price, and counterfeiting is a real problem. Shrink bands, induction seals, or tamper-evident closures are essential for retail organic products to assure consumers they are getting genuine certified organic food.

Poor label legibility: Cramming all the required information (FSSAI details, organic certification, nutritional info, ingredients, batch codes) onto a small label in tiny font makes the information useless. Design your label to be readable at normal viewing distance. If the primary packaging is too small, use a secondary carton or a fold-out label.

Cost Considerations for Organic Food Packaging

Organic food packaging typically costs 15-40% more than equivalent packaging for conventional food products. The premium comes from several factors: food-grade material requirements, additional labelling needs (Jaivik Bharat logo, certification details), smaller print runs (organic brands tend to be smaller-scale), and the preference for premium materials like glass and kraft paper.

For startups and smaller organic brands, managing packaging costs requires pragmatic choices. Use standard stock packaging in standard sizes rather than custom-moulded containers. Choose sticker labels over direct printing for small runs, as the per-unit cost of printing plates only makes sense above 10,000-20,000 units. Start with the minimum packaging that meets regulatory requirements and protects the product, then upgrade aesthetics as volumes grow.

Packaging as a Trust Signal

In the organic food market, packaging serves a dual role. It protects the product physically, but it also communicates trust. Consumers pay a premium for organic food because they trust the certification, the supply chain, and the brand. Packaging that looks cheap, is poorly labelled, or uses materials inconsistent with organic values erodes that trust.

Invest in clean, professional label design. Make the Jaivik Bharat logo and organic certification prominent, not hidden in a corner. Choose materials that feel substantial. A heavy glass jar or a thick kraft pouch communicates quality before the customer even opens the product.

For food service and delivery businesses, branded packaging with clear organic messaging differentiates your organic meals from the hundreds of other delivery options on Swiggy and Zomato.
