Wax-Coated Paper for Food Packaging: Benefits and Complete Guide

July 8, 2025 13 min read Eco-Friendly

Before plastic wrap and aluminium foil became ubiquitous, wax-coated paper was the standard method for keeping food fresh and protected. Today, as Indian food businesses seek alternatives to plastic wrapping and lining materials, wax paper is experiencing a significant revival. It offers grease resistance, moisture protection, and a natural feel that aligns with both regulatory trends and customer preferences -- without the environmental guilt of plastic film.

This guide examines the types of wax coatings used in food packaging, their properties, safety compliance in the Indian context, practical applications, and how they compare with competing materials.

What Is Wax-Coated Paper?

Wax-coated paper (commonly called wax paper) is produced by applying a thin layer of wax to one or both sides of food-grade base paper. The wax penetrates the paper fibres and forms a continuous surface film, creating a barrier against moisture and grease while maintaining the paper's flexibility and foldability.

The base paper is typically 30-60 GSM tissue paper, MG (Machine Glazed) kraft, or parchment paper. The wax coating adds 8-15 GSM of material weight. The resulting product is lightweight, conformable, and provides moderate barrier properties sufficient for most short-contact food wrapping and lining applications.

Paraffin Wax -- The Industry Standard

Paraffin wax accounts for over 80% of wax-coated food paper produced in India. It is inexpensive, readily available, and provides good moisture and grease barrier properties. Food-grade paraffin wax (complying with IS 4654 and FDA 21 CFR 178.3710) is refined to remove impurities and is considered safe for food contact applications where the food temperature does not exceed the wax melting point.

The key limitation is its low melting point. At temperatures above 60-65 degrees Celsius, paraffin wax softens and can transfer to food, creating both an aesthetic issue (waxy feel on food) and a potential ingestion concern at high doses. For this reason, paraffin wax paper is not recommended for hot food wrapping -- it is best suited for room-temperature and cold food applications.

Soy Wax -- The Sustainable Alternative

Soy wax is derived from hydrogenated soybean oil. It offers comparable barrier properties to paraffin at a 30-50% price premium. The key advantage is environmental: soy wax is biodegradable, compostable, and derived from a renewable agricultural crop. India's growing soybean production (particularly in Madhya Pradesh and Maharashtra) supports domestic supply.

Soy wax paper is increasingly specified by food businesses targeting eco-conscious consumers and seeking compostable packaging certifications. Its slightly higher melting range also provides marginally better heat tolerance.

Beeswax -- The Premium Option

Beeswax-coated paper and beeswax wraps (reusable cloths coated with beeswax, tree resin, and jojoba oil) occupy the premium end of the market. They offer excellent moisture resistance, natural antimicrobial properties, and a pleasant honey scent. In the food business context, beeswax wraps are more relevant for retail (cheese shops, organic stores) than for high-volume food service. Cost is 3-5 times higher than paraffin equivalents.

Applications in Indian Food Service

Burger and Sandwich Wrapping

Wax paper is the classic material for wrapping burgers, sandwiches, wraps, and rolls. It holds the food together during eating, absorbs excess grease, and prevents the filling from soaking through to the customer's hands. For QSR chains and street food vendors, wax-coated wrapping sheets (typically 25x30 cm or 30x35 cm) are a staple inventory item. The semi-transparent quality of wax paper also allows partial food visibility, which aids in order identification during busy service.

Basket and Tray Liners

Wax paper liners are placed in food baskets and trays to catch grease from fried items -- french fries, onion rings, pakoras, and samosas. They serve a dual function: keeping the basket clean for reuse and providing a hygienic surface between the basket and the food. Pre-cut square or rectangular liners in checked (red/white or red) patterns are a distinctive visual element of fast food presentation.

Bakery Interleaving

Sheets of wax paper placed between layers of baked goods prevent sticking and absorb excess surface moisture. This is standard practice for stacking cookies, pastries, naan bread, and rotis. Bakeries that pack boxes with multiple items use wax paper interleaving to maintain product quality and presentation.

Cheese and Dairy Wrapping

Wax paper's moisture-regulating properties make it suitable for wrapping cheese, paneer, and butter. Unlike plastic cling wrap (which traps moisture and promotes bacterial growth), wax paper allows controlled moisture exchange, keeping dairy products fresh without sogginess. This application is particularly relevant for dairy shops, sweet shops, and deli counters.

Street Food Packaging

India's street food culture extensively uses paper-based wrapping, and wax-coated varieties offer an upgrade from plain newsprint (which is not food-safe due to ink contamination). Wax paper provides grease resistance for chaat plates, pav bhaji servings, rolls, and similar street food items. For more on this category, see our street food packaging guide.

Food Safety and Regulatory Status

Wax-coated paper for food contact must satisfy several regulatory requirements in India:

  • Base paper: Must be virgin pulp (not recycled) conforming to IS 6608 for food contact paper. Lead, cadmium, and total heavy metal limits apply.
  • Wax quality: Food-grade paraffin wax must conform to IS 4654 (Specification for Paraffin Wax, Food Grade). Key parameters include oil content (maximum 0.5%), colour (Saybolt colour 25 minimum), and absence of polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH).
  • Overall migration: The combined paper-and-wax system must not exceed 60 mg/kg overall migration when tested per IS 9845 with appropriate food simulants.
  • Printing: Any printing on the food-contact side must use food-safe inks per IS 15495. Ideally, printing should be on the non-contact (outer) side only.

One important note: the red-and-white checkered "food paper" widely sold in Indian wholesale markets is not always food-grade certified. The red dye used in cheap versions may contain non-food-safe pigments. Always verify that your supplier can provide FSSAI-compliant documentation for coloured or printed wax papers.

Limitations and Best Practices

Wax paper is not a universal solution. It should not be used for:

  • Wrapping very hot food (above 60 degrees Celsius for paraffin; above 65 degrees for soy wax)
  • Lining containers for liquids or gravies (wax coating is not waterproof, only water-resistant)
  • Microwave reheating (wax melts and can ignite at high temperatures)
  • Long-term food storage exceeding 4-6 hours (barrier properties degrade over time)

For hot food applications, greaseproof paper or parchment paper are better alternatives. For liquid-containing foods, PE-coated or PLA-coated kraft paper products are the appropriate choice.

Sourcing and Cost Tips

Wax paper is among the most affordable food packaging materials. Standard paraffin-coated food wrapping paper retails at Rs 80-150 per kg at wholesale (which translates to approximately 200-400 sheets of standard wrapping size). For volume buyers, costs drop further:

  • Reels: For high-volume operations, purchasing wax paper in reels (15-25 kg) and cutting to size in-house reduces per-sheet cost by 20-30%.
  • Pre-cut sheets: More convenient for smaller operations. Available in standard sizes or custom-cut to your specifications.
  • Printed variants: Custom-printed wax paper adds Rs 2-4 per sheet at volumes of 10,000+ sheets, providing branding at minimal incremental cost.

Storage is straightforward: keep wax paper in a cool, dry area away from direct heat sources (which can cause sheets to stick together). Shelf life is effectively unlimited when stored properly, making it a low-risk inventory item.
