Deep Containers for Gravy Dishes: Packaging Indian Curries Right

May 18, 2025 14 min read Containers

Indian cuisine runs on gravy. From the butter chicken that dominates Delhi delivery orders to the fish curry that defines coastal kitchens from Goa to Kerala, gravies are the soul of most Indian meals. They are also, without question, the most difficult category of food to package for delivery.

A thin, watery curry in a shallow container is a leak waiting to happen. A thick, rich gravy like shahi paneer packed in a container that is too wide loses heat quickly because of the large surface area exposed to the lid. And any Indian gravy packed with insufficient headspace will push its lid off the moment the delivery bag tilts on a bumpy road.

Deep containers — taller than they are wide, with a depth-to-width ratio that favours vertical capacity over horizontal spread — are the solution that serious food businesses are adopting for gravy delivery. This guide explains why depth matters, how to choose the right deep container for different Indian gravies, and what to look for when ordering in bulk.

Why Depth Matters for Gravy Packaging

The depth of a food container affects three critical factors for gravy delivery:

Heat Retention

Physics works in your favour with deep containers. A tall, narrow container has a smaller surface-area-to-volume ratio than a shallow, wide container holding the same amount. Less surface area means less heat escapes to the surrounding air. A 500 ml gravy in a deep container retains measurably more heat over a 30-minute delivery window than the same 500 ml in a shallow container. For Indian customers who expect their butter chicken piping hot, this matters enormously.

Leak Resistance

A deeper container provides more headspace above the gravy level. When the container tilts during transit, the gravy sloshes up the walls rather than pushing against the lid seal. With a 3-inch-deep container filled to the 2-inch mark, you have a full inch of buffer before the gravy even contacts the lid. With a shallow container filled to near the rim, any tilt sends gravy directly against the lid, testing the seal with every bump in the road.

Portion Perception

A deep container with a moderate opening looks generous even with a standard portion. The customer peers into the container and sees depth, which the brain interprets as quantity. The same volume in a wide, shallow container looks spread thin and unimpressive. For gravies especially, where the richness of the dish is part of its value, the deep presentation reinforces the perception of a hearty, substantial portion.

Indian Gravies and Their Container Requirements

Not all gravies are the same. Consistency, temperature, oil content, and typical portion size all influence which deep container works best. Here is a breakdown by gravy type:

Rich, Thick Gravies

Butter chicken, paneer butter masala, dal makhani, malai kofta, and shahi korma are thick, creamy preparations with significant oil or butter content. These gravies are viscous enough that they do not slosh aggressively during transit, but the oil can separate and pool at the surface, creating a greasy film on the lid. Deep containers with non-stick-friendly surfaces (PP is ideal) handle these well. The depth keeps the gravy compact, and the smaller opening minimises the oil film area.

Medium Gravies

Chole, rajma, egg curry, chicken do pyaza, and most vegetable curries have a moderate consistency. They flow more than thick gravies but are not watery. These are the most common gravy type in Indian delivery and work well in standard deep containers with snap-fit lids. The key requirement is a lid that seals reliably, because medium-consistency gravies generate enough pressure during transit to exploit any weakness in the seal.

Thin, Watery Gravies

Rasam, kadhi, sambar (thin versions), mutton shorba, and certain Goan curries are quite liquid. These require the deepest containers with the most secure lids. Screw-thread lids or heat-sealed lids are recommended for thin gravies that will travel more than 15-20 minutes. If using snap-fit lids, secure them with tape or a rubber band as additional insurance.

Material Recommendations for Gravy Containers

PP (Polypropylene) — The Best All-Rounder

PP handles the high temperatures of freshly cooked Indian gravies (often served above 80 degrees Celsius) without warping, is resistant to turmeric staining, does not react with the acidic tomato bases common in North Indian curries, and produces a clean rim for reliable lid sealing. It is also microwave-safe, which is a significant advantage since most delivered gravies need reheating.

Aluminium — Best for Heat Retention

If your priority is keeping the gravy hot for as long as possible, aluminium deep containers are unmatched. The metal conducts and retains heat far better than plastic. This makes aluminium the preferred choice for catering deliveries, long-distance orders, and any situation where the gravy must arrive genuinely hot, not just warm. The downside is that aluminium is not microwave-safe and the crimped lid seal is less reliable for very liquid gravies.

Paper with Lining — Premium Positioning

Lined paper deep containers project a premium image and provide good insulation. They are popular with higher-end restaurants and cloud kitchens targeting customers who order Rs 300+ gravies. The insulation means the outer surface stays cool enough to handle comfortably, which improves the unboxing experience.

Lid Sealing Strategies for Gravy Containers

The lid is where most gravy delivery failures happen. Here is a ranked approach to sealing deep gravy containers, from most to least effective:

  1. Heat-sealed film + snap-fit lid: A heat-sealed plastic film over the container opening provides a leak-proof primary seal. A snap-fit lid placed over the film adds insulation and a professional appearance. This dual-seal approach is used by top-rated cloud kitchens and virtually eliminates leaks. Requires a lid-sealing machine (Rs 15,000-40,000 investment).
  2. Snap-fit lid + tape seal: A secure snap-fit lid reinforced with a strip of tape across the opening. The tape adds leak protection and provides tamper evidence. Cost-effective and suitable for most operations.
  3. Screw-thread lid: Available on some paper and PP containers. Provides excellent sealing for liquid gravies. Slower to close but very reliable.
  4. Snap-fit lid only: Adequate for thick gravies and shorter delivery distances. Check that the snap tabs engage fully at all points around the rim.
  5. Press-fit lid: The least secure option. Use only for very thick gravies (like dry mutton curry or bhuna dishes) that do not flow freely.

Packing Gravies for Delivery: Step-by-Step

Getting the packing process right is just as important as choosing the right container. Here is the workflow that minimises spills and customer complaints:

  1. Let the gravy cool for 2-3 minutes after cooking. This reduces steam buildup in the sealed container and gives the gravy a slightly thicker consistency that is less prone to sloshing.
  2. Ladle the gravy into the deep container, filling to no more than 80% of the container's depth. The remaining 20% is your transit buffer.
  3. Wipe the container rim with a clean, dry cloth. Any gravy on the rim will prevent the lid from sealing properly.
  4. Place the lid and press firmly until you hear or feel the snap tabs click at all points. For liquid gravies, apply tape across the lid.
  5. Place the sealed container upright in the delivery bag. Position it at the bottom of the bag, surrounded by other containers that provide lateral support.
  6. If the order includes multiple gravy containers, place them side by side rather than stacking them. Stacking puts pressure on the lower container's lid.

Common Gravy Packaging Mistakes

  • Using shallow containers for thin gravies: This is the number one cause of gravy leaks during delivery. A 250 ml kadhi in a shallow 300 ml container has almost no headspace. Switch to a 400 ml deep container for the same portion.
  • Filling to the brim: Kitchen staff naturally want to fill containers generously, but a full-to-the-top gravy container is a leak guarantee. Train your team to fill to the 80% mark consistently.
  • Ignoring rim cleanliness: A single drop of gravy on the rim can prevent the lid from sealing. It takes 2 seconds to wipe and saves hours of complaint handling.
  • Sealing too quickly after cooking: Boiling gravy sealed immediately creates steam pressure inside the container. The steam pushes the lid continuously during transit. A 2-3 minute cool-down eliminates this problem.
  • Not testing with actual foods: Different gravies behave differently in the same container. Oily gravies may not leak but stain lids. Acidic gravies may affect certain materials. Test your actual menu items before ordering in bulk.

Cost Considerations

Deep containers typically cost 10-20% more than equivalent-volume shallow containers because they use more material per unit. However, the reduction in gravy spill complaints, which often require a free replacement meal costing Rs 150-300, makes this investment worthwhile. A single avoided spill complaint pays for the price difference across 30-50 containers.

At wholesale volumes of 2,000+ pieces, deep PP containers in the popular 300-500 ml range are available at Rs 3.00 to Rs 5.50 per piece including lid. For a restaurant spending Rs 15-20 on total packaging per order, upgrading from shallow to deep gravy containers adds less than Rs 1 per order.
