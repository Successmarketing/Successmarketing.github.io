Food Packaging Market in Ahmedabad: Sourcing Guide for Gujarat's Food Hub

June 20, 2025 14 min read Industry

Ahmedabad does not do things halfway. This is a city where a simple breakfast of fafda-jalebi on a Sunday morning is a citywide ritual, where the Manek Chowk night market turns a jewellery bazaar into a food carnival after dark, and where a Gujarati thali is not a meal but a declaration of abundance with fifteen-plus items on a single plate. The food culture here is large, generous, and deeply rooted in community. And the packaging industry that supports it reflects exactly those qualities.

Gujarat's commercial capital is home to an estimated 35,000-40,000 food establishments. From the iconic Gujarati thali restaurants like Agashiye and Vishalla to the street food stalls of Law Garden and Manek Chowk, from the farsan shops that supply namkeen across India to the growing cloud kitchen sector in areas like Satellite, Prahlad Nagar, and SG Highway, Ahmedabad's food industry is vast and varied. Each of these businesses relies on packaging, and the market has evolved to meet demand at every scale and price point.

Ahmedabad's Wholesale Packaging Markets

Revdi Bazaar and Relief Road Area

The old city area around Revdi Bazaar, Relief Road, and Kalupur is Ahmedabad's traditional wholesale hub for packaging materials. This is where packaging traders have operated for decades, and the market offers the broadest selection of products in the city. Paper cups, plates, aluminium containers, sweet boxes, bags, and wrapping materials are all available from multiple competing shops within walking distance of each other.

Revdi Bazaar is particularly strong for paper products and boxes. The area has direct connections to Gujarat's paper industry, and the shops here carry everything from basic unprinted paper cups to custom-printed packaging for established brands. Pricing is competitive, and because of the concentration of shops, you can compare quotes within an hour and make informed purchasing decisions.

Vatva and Narol Industrial Areas

The Vatva GIDC and Narol industrial areas are where many of Ahmedabad's packaging manufacturers are based. Paper cup manufacturing, plastic container moulding, aluminium foil converting, and paper plate pressing units all operate here. For bulk buyers ordering 10,000 pieces or more, going directly to Vatva or Narol can cut 15-25% off wholesale market prices.

Gujarat has a strong manufacturing base for food packaging, and Ahmedabad is its centre. Several Vatva-based manufacturers supply nationally, which means you get access to products that meet national quality standards at local prices. The challenge is that manufacturers focus on production and may not offer the same customer service experience as a dedicated wholesale supplier.

Naroda Industrial Area

Naroda, on the eastern outskirts of Ahmedabad, is another industrial cluster with packaging units. This area has seen growth in eco-friendly packaging manufacturing, with units producing bagasse plates and bowls, paper straws, and biodegradable containers. For Ahmedabad businesses looking to adopt sustainable packaging, Naroda is worth exploring.

What Ahmedabad's Food Businesses Need

Gujarati Thali Restaurants

The Gujarati thali is a packaging challenge of the first order. A standard thali includes dal, kadhi, two or three sabzis, roti or puri, rice, papad, pickle, salad, a farsan item, and a sweet. That is ten to fifteen distinct items that need to reach the customer without mixing, without spilling, and preferably still warm.

For dine-in, the stainless steel thali plate is standard. But for delivery, which now accounts for a growing share of even traditional thali restaurants' revenue, the options are compartment plates with five to seven sections, or individual small containers bundled together. The compartment plate approach is more cost-effective at Rs 4-8 per plate versus Rs 20-30 for a set of individual containers, but the individual container approach keeps items better separated and allows for reheating individual components.

Most Ahmedabad thali delivery services have settled on a hybrid approach: compartment plates for dry items like roti, papad, and dry sabzis, combined with small sealed containers for liquids like dal, kadhi, and wet sabzis. This balances cost and functionality effectively.

Farsan and Namkeen Businesses

Ahmedabad is the farsan capital of India. Fafda, gathiya, chakli, sev, khakhra, and dozens of other varieties are produced and consumed in enormous quantities. The packaging requirements for farsan are specific: airtight sealing to maintain crispness, oil-resistant materials because most farsan items are fried, and protective structure to prevent breakage during transport.

For retail farsan shops, the typical packaging includes food-grade plastic pouches with zip locks or heat sealing for loose farsan, cardboard boxes with plastic or wax-paper lining for gift packs and assortments, small PP containers with lids for items like dhokla and khandvi that need to stay moist, and paper cones and wraps for freshly fried items like fafda and gathiya sold for immediate consumption.

Ahmedabad's farsan businesses are some of the most cost-conscious packaging buyers in the food industry. They deal in volumes where even half a paisa per piece matters. Wholesale buying in quantities of 25,000 to 50,000 pieces at a time is common for established farsan chains.

Street Food Vendors

Ahmedabad's street food scene is vibrant. The famous night market at Manek Chowk, the stalls along Law Garden, the khau galli areas near Kankaria Lake, and the Saturday market at Khamasa Circle all rely on simple, affordable packaging. Paper plates for dabeli and pav bhaji, small bowls for dhokla and khandvi, paper cups for buttermilk and masala chaas, and paper cones for bhelpuri and sev puri.

Street food packaging in Ahmedabad runs under Rs 1.50 per serving. At these price points, every paisa matters, and buying wholesale in bulk is the only way to keep costs manageable. A busy street food stall going through 300-500 plates per day can save Rs 3,000-5,000 per month by buying at wholesale rates versus retail.

Sweet Shops

Gujaratis have a famously sweet tooth, and Ahmedabad's mithai shops are legion. From the famous Kandoi shops to neighbourhood sweet shops in every locality, the demand for sweet packaging is year-round, with massive spikes during Diwali, Uttarayan (Makar Sankranti), and the wedding season.

Sweet boxes in Ahmedabad range from basic Rs 5-10 boxes for everyday sales to premium Rs 50-150 gift boxes for festival gifting. The Diwali season alone can see a single medium-sized sweet shop going through 5,000-10,000 boxes. Early ordering, ideally 4-6 weeks before Diwali, is essential to secure supply and favourable pricing.

Climate Considerations for Ahmedabad

Ahmedabad's climate is harsh. Summer temperatures routinely exceed 43 degrees Celsius, with May and June being particularly brutal. This extreme heat affects packaging in several ways:

  • Container warping: Low-quality plastic containers can warp when filled with hot food on a 45-degree day. Use PP containers rated for high temperatures, and avoid PS (polystyrene) during summer months.
  • Lid seal failure: Heat expansion can cause snap-fit lids to pop open. During summer, containers with more secure closure mechanisms, crimped aluminium lids or threaded plastic caps, perform better than simple press-fit lids.
  • Paper degradation: Paper products stored in non-air-conditioned spaces can become brittle in extreme dry heat. Keep paper cup and plate inventory in a cool, covered area away from direct sunlight.
  • Food safety urgency: In Ahmedabad's heat, food spoilage risk is high. Sealed, food-grade packaging is not just a preference; it is a food safety necessity. Open or poorly sealed packaging in 45-degree heat can allow bacterial growth within hours.

During the monsoon months, July through September, humidity replaces heat as the primary concern. Paper products absorb moisture, farsan loses crunch, and sweet packaging can develop condensation on the inside. Moisture-proof packaging and rapid inventory turnover are critical during this period.

Packaging Regulations in Gujarat

Gujarat has implemented the central government's single-use plastic ban, prohibiting specific categories of thin plastic items. The Gujarat Pollution Control Board (GPCB) conducts enforcement drives, particularly in commercial areas and food markets. Ahmedabad Municipal Corporation (AMC) has been active in enforcing the ban in market areas like Manek Chowk, Law Garden, and the old city bazaars.

For Ahmedabad food businesses, the compliance requirements are clear: no plastic bags below 75 microns, no plastic plates or cups of banned categories, no thermocol packaging. The compliant alternatives, paper, aluminium, PP above specified thickness, bagasse, and other biodegradable materials, are all widely available in Ahmedabad's wholesale markets at reasonable prices.
