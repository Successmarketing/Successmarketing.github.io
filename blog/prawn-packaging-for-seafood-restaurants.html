Prawn Packaging for Seafood Restaurants: From Curry to Tandoori to Fried

May 14, 2025 13 min read Food Packaging

Prawns occupy a premium position on Indian restaurant menus. Whether it is the coconut-milk-based prawn curry of Kerala, the butter garlic prawns popular in Goa, the tandoori jumbo prawns served in North Indian restaurants, or the crispy fried prawns from Bengal, these dishes command higher prices and carry higher customer expectations. A prawn dish that arrives poorly packaged does not just result in a bad review. It represents a significant financial loss because the ingredient cost of prawns is two to three times higher than chicken or mutton per kilogram.

The packaging challenges with prawns are distinct from other non-veg items. Prawns are small, delicate, and come in preparations that range from dry to swimming in gravy. The shell-on variants can puncture thin containers. The gravies are thinner and more prone to spilling than thick chicken curries. And the smell of improperly sealed prawn packaging can alienate customers faster than almost any other food item.

Understanding Prawn Packaging Challenges

Prawn dishes present several packaging challenges that restaurant owners must address:

Thin, hot gravies. Unlike a thick butter chicken where the gravy coats the meat and stays relatively contained, prawn curries often have thinner, more liquid gravies. Coconut-based prawn curries, Goan xacuti, and Mangalorean prawn gassi all have a sauce consistency closer to a soup than a paste. This liquid sloshes around during delivery and exploits any weakness in the container seal. Even a tiny gap in the lid can result in a catastrophic spill.

Shell-on preparations. Many popular prawn dishes in India are prepared with the shell on, especially tandoori prawns and certain regional curries. The sharp points of prawn shells, particularly the head and tail sections, can puncture thin aluminium lids and the walls of budget containers. We have seen cases where a single prawn tail spike has poked through a lid, creating a leak point that drains the entire gravy during transit.

Small, dispersed pieces. Unlike a single large piece of tandoori chicken that sits stable in a container, a prawn order consists of multiple small pieces floating in gravy or scattered across a plate. During delivery, these pieces shift, settle, and can pile up on one side if the container tilts. This affects both presentation and temperature consistency.

Rapid temperature drop. Prawns are smaller than chicken or mutton pieces, which means they have a higher surface-area-to-volume ratio. This causes them to cool faster. A prawn curry that leaves the kitchen at 80 degrees can drop to 55 degrees in 25 minutes, which is faster than most other non-veg curries.

Strong odour. Prawn dishes have a distinctive seafood smell that intensifies when sealed in a container. While the aroma is appealing during dining, a sealed container concentrates the smell, and if it leaks, the odour transfers to carry bags, delivery bags, and other items in the same order. Odour containment is critical for prawn packaging.

Container Selection by Prawn Preparation Type

Prawn Curry and Gravy Dishes

For gravy-based prawn preparations like prawn masala, prawn curry, Goan prawn vindaloo, or Kerala prawn moilee, the container must be absolutely leak-proof. There is no margin for error here. A leaking prawn curry container ruins everything it touches.

The best options for prawn curry packaging are:

  • Deep PP containers with snap-lock lids: Polypropylene containers with a positive locking mechanism (not just press-fit) are the most reliable for thin gravies. The snap-lock creates a mechanical seal that resists the sloshing forces during delivery. Choose containers in the 400-600 ml range for single-serve orders.
  • Aluminium containers with crimped lids plus cling wrap: If you prefer aluminium, apply a layer of cling wrap over the container opening before crimping the lid. The cling wrap provides a secondary seal that catches any leakage past the crimped edge. This double-seal approach is highly effective for gravy dishes.

Tandoori and Grilled Prawns

Tandoori prawns and grilled prawn preparations are dry or semi-dry, with oil and marinade residue rather than a flowing gravy. The packaging needs are similar to tandoori chicken: prevent sogginess from steam condensation while retaining heat.

Use shallow, wide containers rather than deep ones. Tandoori prawns look best when arranged in a single layer, and the shallow container reduces the distance steam must travel before hitting the lid and condensing. Line the container base with butter paper to absorb the oil and marinade that drips from the prawns.

For jumbo tandoori prawns, which are a high-value order, consider using a premium presentation box with a clear window lid. The visual impact of seeing the charred, orange-red prawns through the window enhances the unboxing experience and justifies the premium price.

Fried Prawns and Prawn Koliwada

Fried prawn preparations like prawn koliwada (a Mumbai favourite), crispy fried prawns, and prawn tempura need packaging that preserves crispness. The approach is similar to fish fry packaging: ventilation, absorbent liners, and minimal contact between pieces.

Use a ventilated container or a paper-lined kraft box. Place a layer of absorbent tissue at the bottom and arrange the fried prawns in a single layer. If the order has more pieces than can fit in one layer, separate the layers with butter paper. Never pack fried prawns tightly; the trapped steam between densely packed pieces will destroy the crispy coating within minutes.

Prawn Biryani

Prawn biryani combines the challenges of biryani packaging with the specific issues of prawn packaging. The rice releases steam, the prawns cool quickly, and the thin prawn stock at the bottom is prone to leaking. Use the same container approach as chicken biryani, heavy-gauge aluminium or PP with a secure lid, but step down one size since prawn biryani servings are typically lighter than chicken biryani.

A 600-750 ml container works well for a single-serve prawn biryani. Make sure the prawns are distributed throughout the rice, not piled on top, as surface-exposed prawns will cool and dry out faster than those nestled within the rice.

Leak Prevention: The Critical Factor

We cannot overstate how important leak prevention is for prawn packaging. A leaking prawn curry container is not just a mess; the smell of spilled prawn gravy in a delivery bag is almost impossible to remove and can ruin the rider's bag for subsequent deliveries. Delivery platforms have received complaints from riders about this specific issue, and some riders refuse prawn orders from restaurants with a history of leakage.

Here is a leak-prevention protocol that works:

  1. Choose the right container: PP containers with snap-lock lids for curries. Heavy aluminium with crimped lids for dry preparations.
  2. Fill to 80% capacity: Leave headroom for the gravy to move without reaching the seal line. Overfilled containers are the primary cause of leakage.
  3. Apply cling wrap before the lid: For any gravy-based dish, stretch cling wrap tightly over the container opening before placing the lid. This adds a waterproof barrier.
  4. Test the seal: Before bagging, briefly tilt the sealed container to 45 degrees and check for any leakage. Catch the leak in the kitchen, not in the customer's lap.
  5. Use an inner carry bag: Place the sealed container inside a knotted plastic bag before putting it in the delivery bag. This provides a third layer of leak protection.

Temperature Retention for Prawn Dishes

Prawns lose heat faster than larger pieces of meat. Here are strategies to keep prawn dishes warm during delivery:

  • Use insulated containers: Double-wall PP containers or containers wrapped in aluminium foil retain heat significantly better than single-wall alternatives.
  • Pack the gravy hot: For curry dishes, heat the gravy to near-boiling before pouring it over the prawns in the container. The thermal mass of the hot gravy helps maintain temperature.
  • Group prawn orders: If a delivery includes a prawn dish along with other items, place the prawn container in the centre of the bag, surrounded by other hot items. The peripheral items act as insulation.
  • Insulated bags: Invest in insulated delivery bags with reflective linings. These bags extend the food's temperature window by 15-20 minutes, which is significant for quick-cooling prawn dishes.

Presentation and Branding for Premium Prawn Orders

Prawn dishes are premium items on any menu. A Rs 400-600 prawn masala packed in a flimsy, unmarked container creates a value disconnect that damages your brand. Invest in packaging that matches the price point:

  • Branded containers or stickers: At minimum, apply a branded sticker to every prawn dish container. The sticker should include your restaurant name, logo, and a contact number for reorders.
  • Clear-lid containers: Where practical, use containers with transparent lids. Customers appreciate being able to see the prawns, especially for premium orders.
  • Portion presentation: Arrange prawns neatly in the container rather than dumping them in. Place garnish (coriander leaves, lemon wedge, fried curry leaves) on top for visual appeal when the customer opens the container.

Seafood Packaging Compliance in India

FSSAI has specific guidelines for seafood packaging that go beyond general food packaging requirements:

  • Seafood must be packed in food-grade containers that do not react with the acids naturally present in seafood preparations (tamarind, kokum, vinegar, and lemon are common in prawn dishes).
  • Packaging must maintain food at safe temperatures. For hot prawn dishes, this means above 63 degrees Celsius during the intended consumption window.
  • Cross-contamination prevention is mandatory. Prawn dishes must be packaged separately from other food items, especially vegetarian items, within the same order.
  • FSSAI licence number must appear on the packaging, either printed or on a sticker.

Ordering Prawn Packaging in Bulk

Seafood restaurants with steady prawn dish sales should order packaging in bulk to secure the best per-unit pricing. Here are some practical tips:

  • Order leak-proof containers in lots of 500-1,000 to access wholesale rates. Buying 100 at a time from local retailers costs 25-40% more per unit.
  • Stock different container sizes: 300-400 ml for prawn starters and appetisers, 500-600 ml for main course curries, and 750 ml+ for prawn biryani.
  • Keep separate container stock for dry and gravy preparations. Using a gravy-grade container for fried prawns is wasteful; using a basic container for prawn curry risks leakage.
  • Always test a new container with your actual prawn curry before committing to a bulk order. Fill it, seal it, turn it upside down for 5 minutes. If even a drop leaks, reject that container.
