Cling Film and Food Wrap: The Complete Usage Guide for Commercial Kitchens

July 15, 2025 11 min read Cutlery

In any commercial kitchen -- whether it is a busy restaurant in Kota, a cloud kitchen in Jaipur, or a five-star hotel kitchen in Mumbai -- cling film is used dozens of times every day. Covering prep bowls, wrapping cut fruits and vegetables, sealing marinating containers, protecting plated dishes during transit -- cling film is the invisible workhorse that keeps food fresh and prevents contamination. Yet most kitchen managers buy cling film without understanding the differences between types, the food safety implications, or how to use it most effectively. This guide changes that.

What Exactly is Cling Film?

Cling film (also called cling wrap, plastic wrap, or food wrap) is a thin, transparent plastic film that stretches and clings to surfaces, creating an airtight seal. Its "cling" property comes from the material's electrostatic charge and, in some types, added plasticizers that make it tacky.

There are two main types of cling film available in the Indian market, and understanding the difference is crucial for food safety.

FSSAI Recommendation

The Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) advises against using PVC cling film for wrapping food items, particularly fatty and oily foods, and food that will be heated. FSSAI recommends using cling film made from LDPE or other food-safe plastics that do not contain harmful plasticizers. When purchasing cling film, check the packaging for material specification and food-grade certification.

When to Use Cling Film (and When Not To)

Ideal Uses

  • Covering bowls and containers: The primary use. Stretch cling film over bowls of prepped ingredients, marinating dishes, cut fruits, and salad components to keep them fresh in the refrigerator.
  • Wrapping cut produce: Half-used onions, lemons, tomatoes, and other cut vegetables stay fresh longer when wrapped tightly in cling film.
  • Protecting plated dishes: In catering, cling-wrapped plates can be prepared in advance and stored until service time.
  • Dough proofing: Cover dough balls or trays with cling film during proofing to prevent surface drying. It is more effective than a damp cloth because it creates a sealed environment.
  • Layering and stacking: Use cling film between stacked items (like roti, parantha, or dough sheets) to prevent sticking.
  • Sealing containers for transport: Before putting lids on takeaway containers, stretch cling film across the top for an extra layer of leak protection.

When to Avoid Cling Film

  • Direct contact with hot food: Never place cling film directly on food that is still hot. Allow food to cool to room temperature first, or leave a gap between the film and the food surface.
  • In the oven or tandoor: Cling film melts at relatively low temperatures (around 100-120 degrees Celsius). Never use it in conventional ovens, tandoors, or on hot stovetops.
  • In the microwave (with PVC): If using PVC cling film, do not microwave. LDPE cling film can be used for brief microwave reheating at medium power, but ensure the film does not touch the food directly.
  • Long-term storage of fatty food: For extended storage (more than 24 hours) of cheese, butter, fatty meats, or oily food, use glass or stainless steel containers instead. Plasticizers (especially in PVC) can leach into fatty foods over time.
  • Freezer storage (long-term): For freezing food for more than a week, use freezer bags or aluminum foil instead. Standard cling film becomes brittle and cracks at freezer temperatures, compromising the seal. Learn more about foil options in our aluminum foil guide.

Cling Film in Commercial Kitchen Operations

Prep Station

A commercial kitchen's prep station is where cling film sees its heaviest use. Every prepped ingredient that is not being used immediately should be covered. This is not just about freshness -- FSSAI and local health inspectors check for proper food covering during kitchen audits. Uncovered food in a walk-in cooler or on a prep table is a health code violation in most jurisdictions.

For efficient prep station use:

  • Mount a cling film dispenser at the prep counter for quick, one-handed operation
  • Use the dispenser's built-in cutter for clean cuts (tearing by hand wastes film and creates uneven edges)
  • Stretch the film tight -- loose film does not create a proper seal
  • Label covered items with the date and contents using a marker on masking tape

Cold Storage

In walk-in coolers and reach-in refrigerators, cling film serves multiple purposes:

  • Prevents moisture loss (wilting, drying) from stored foods
  • Prevents cross-contamination between stored items
  • Prevents odor transfer between strong-smelling items (onions, garlic, fish) and neutral items (milk, cream, desserts)
  • Allows visual identification of contents without opening containers

Catering and Event Service

For catering operations, cling film is essential for:

  • Pre-wrapping individual plates and bowls hours before an event
  • Covering large serving trays during transport
  • Sealing chafing dish inserts before placing in hot water baths
  • Wrapping buffet items between service rounds to maintain freshness

Commercial kitchen rolls typically come in lengths of 100, 300, or 600 meters. The 300-meter roll in 30-45 cm width is the most popular for mid-sized restaurants. Larger kitchens and caterers prefer 600-meter rolls for better per-meter economics.

Tips for Reducing Cling Film Waste

Cling film is a single-use plastic, and responsible businesses should aim to minimize waste:

  1. Invest in reusable lids: Silicone stretch lids fit over bowls and containers of various sizes. One set of silicone lids can replace thousands of cling film uses for bowl covering.
  2. Use right-sized rolls: If you mostly cover small bowls, a 30 cm roll wastes less per use than a 45 cm roll where you cut off excess.
  3. Train staff on proper technique: One clean pull and stretch is all that is needed. Untrained staff often waste film by pulling too much, crumpling, and re-doing.
  4. Switch to containers with lids: For items stored regularly (like prepped vegetables), investing in lidded containers eliminates cling film use entirely.
  5. Use aluminum foil for items that need oven reheating: This avoids the need to unwrap cling film and re-cover with foil before reheating.
