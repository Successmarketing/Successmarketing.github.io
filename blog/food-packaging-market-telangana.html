Food Packaging Market in Telangana: Hyderabad and Beyond

September 15, 2025 14 min read Industry

Hyderabad is a biryani city. This is not a stereotype; it is a statement of market reality. The dish that was born in the kitchens of the Nizam's court has become an industry in itself, and its packaging requirements have shaped Telangana's entire food packaging market. An estimated 7,500-10,000 restaurants in Hyderabad alone serve biryani as their primary offering, and each one needs containers that can hold a kilogram or more of rice, meat, and gravy without leaking, losing heat, or allowing the aroma to escape during delivery. When you understand Hyderabad's biryani packaging, you understand a significant portion of Telangana's food packaging market.

But Telangana's food story extends well beyond biryani. The state's food packaging market, estimated at Rs 2,800-3,200 crore annually, serves a diverse food ecosystem: the Irani chai cafes that have been a Hyderabad institution for a century, the haleem restaurants that come alive during Ramadan, the Telugu vegetarian restaurants serving meals with pappu, pulusu, and perugu (dal, tamarind curry, and curd), the street food vendors of Charminar, and the growing modern food industry in HITEC City and Gachibowli. Each segment has distinct packaging requirements, and together they create a market that is large, varied, and growing.

Biryani Packaging: The Heart of Hyderabad's Market

Hyderabadi biryani is cooked in dum style, sealed in a pot and slow-cooked so the flavours meld. The customer expectation is that the biryani arrives in a similarly sealed state, with the dum effect preserved until the container is opened at the table. This means the packaging must trap steam and aroma, prevent the rice from drying out, and maintain heat for at least 30-40 minutes after packing.

Aluminium: The Biryani Container Standard

Aluminium containers have become the industry standard for Hyderabadi biryani delivery. The reasons are practical: aluminium conducts heat evenly, so the biryani stays warm throughout the container rather than cooling from the edges inward. The cardboard lid with an aluminium foil lining creates a reasonable seal that traps moisture and aroma. Aluminium containers are stackable, which matters when a delivery partner is carrying five or six biryani orders simultaneously. And the customer can reheat directly in the aluminium container on a stove, which many Hyderabadi households do.

The standard biryani container sizes in Hyderabad are 750 ml for a single serving (roughly 400-500 grams of biryani), 1000-1200 ml for a double serving, and 2000-2500 ml for a family pack. A busy biryani restaurant doing 300 orders per day will consume 300-350 aluminium containers daily, accounting for wastage and test pieces. At wholesale rates of Rs 3-6 per container depending on size, that is Rs 1,000-2,000 per day on the primary container alone. The PP Container Alternative

Some Hyderabad biryani restaurants, particularly those focused on the delivery-app market, have moved to black PP containers with clear or black lids. These are microwave-safe (which aluminium is not), present well in photographs (important for app listings), and can be branded with stickers or printing. The trade-off is that PP does not retain heat as well as aluminium, and the dum effect is harder to maintain. For premium and traditional biryani restaurants, aluminium remains king. For delivery-focused brands competing on price and convenience, PP is gaining ground.

Irani Chai and the Paper Cup Market

Hyderabad's Irani chai culture is unlike any other tea tradition in India. The Irani cafes, many of which have been operating for 80-100 years in the Old City area around Charminar and Nampally, serve a distinctive sweet, milky tea in glass tumblers for dine-in. But for takeaway and the newer chai chains that have sprung up across the city, paper cups are the standard.

The paper cup market in Hyderabad is large because chai consumption is high (Telangana is among the top tea-consuming states in South India), the cutting chai format uses small 65-80 ml cups that are consumed and discarded quickly, and the newer chai chains like Chai Point, Chaayos, and local brands like Tea Time serve hundreds of cups per day per outlet. A medium-sized Irani cafe or chai chain outlet goes through 500-800 paper cups per day. Our paper cups are available in the exact sizes used by Hyderabad's chai and coffee establishments.

Haleem: Seasonal Packaging Demand

Haleem, the slow-cooked meat and wheat porridge that is a Hyderabad speciality, creates a dramatic seasonal packaging demand spike during Ramadan. The dish is sold almost exclusively during the month of Ramadan, and during this period, every restaurant, many hotels, and even dedicated haleem pop-ups serve the dish. The packaging demand during Ramadan is intense: haleem is a thick, heavy preparation that requires sturdy containers, typically in the 300-500 ml range.

The containers must be completely leak-proof because haleem, when warm, becomes semi-liquid and will find any weakness in a seal. PP containers with tight snap-on lids are the standard, with aluminium containers used by premium outlets. During Ramadan, Hyderabad's food packaging consumption can increase by 25-30% compared to regular months, driven almost entirely by haleem packaging demand. Packaging suppliers who serve Hyderabad need to increase stock levels ahead of Ramadan, which shifts dates each year following the Islamic calendar.

HITEC City and the New Hyderabad Food Economy

The western corridor of Hyderabad, from Madhapur through HITEC City to Gachibowli and the Financial District, is India's second-largest IT hub after Bangalore. The workforce here, estimated at over 600,000, has created a food delivery market that is modern, app-driven, and heavily reliant on packaging.

Cloud kitchens in this corridor operate at high density. Madhapur alone has dozens of cloud kitchen facilities, many running four to six brands from a single location. The packaging needs mirror Bangalore's IT corridor: leak-proof containers, microwave-safe materials, branded packaging elements, and tamper-evident seals for delivery platform compliance. The difference is that Hyderabad's food culture means biryani is always the top-selling item, even from cloud kitchens that also serve North Indian, Chinese, and continental food.

For packaging suppliers, the HITEC City corridor represents concentrated, high-volume, quality-conscious demand. These are businesses that will pay a premium for consistent packaging quality because their delivery ratings, and therefore their revenue, depend on it. A cloud kitchen that switches to a cheaper container and sees its spill complaints increase by even 2% will lose far more in refunds and reduced ratings than it saved on packaging.

Wholesale Markets in Hyderabad

Begum Bazaar

Begum Bazaar in the Old City is Hyderabad's traditional wholesale market for packaging materials. The market stretches along the roads near Afzalgunj and Nayapul, with hundreds of shops dealing in paper products, plastic containers, aluminium foil, bags, and general packaging supplies. Begum Bazaar offers the widest selection and most competitive base pricing in the city, but navigating the market requires experience, as product quality varies significantly between traders.

Secunderabad Markets

The General Bazaar and Parklane areas of Secunderabad have wholesale traders who serve the northern and central Hyderabad food businesses. The market here is smaller than Begum Bazaar but more accessible for businesses in Secunderabad, Cantonment, and the newer developments along the Hyderabad-Secunderabad corridor.

Jeedimetla and Medchal Industrial Areas

The industrial areas north of Hyderabad, particularly Jeedimetla, Balanagar, and the Medchal corridor, host packaging manufacturers. Paper cup making units, plastic moulding operations, and aluminium container forming facilities operate here, supplying both the Hyderabad market and other parts of Telangana and Andhra Pradesh. Bulk buyers ordering 10,000+ pieces can source directly from these manufacturers for better pricing.

Beyond Hyderabad: Warangal, Nizamabad, and Karimnagar

Telangana's second-tier cities, Warangal, Nizamabad, and Karimnagar, have food packaging markets that are smaller but growing. These cities have their own food cultures (Warangal's kodi pulusu, Nizamabad's traditional Telugu meals) and their own packaging needs, though on a much smaller scale than Hyderabad.

The wholesale packaging infrastructure in these cities is limited. Most food businesses in second-tier Telangana cities source their packaging either from Hyderabad-based distributors who deliver to the region or from national wholesale suppliers who ship directly. The latter approach is often more convenient and competitive, especially for businesses that need a consistent range of products delivered on a regular schedule.

Regulatory Environment

Telangana has implemented the central single-use plastic ban through the Telangana State Pollution Control Board (TSPCB). Enforcement in Hyderabad is periodic, with drives typically coinciding with festival seasons and special campaigns. The Greater Hyderabad Municipal Corporation (GHMC) conducts checks in commercial areas, particularly in the Old City, Abids, and around major markets.

For food businesses, the compliance path is straightforward: use food-grade materials, avoid banned single-use plastics, and maintain FSSAI credentials on your packaging. The Telangana market has adapted well to the ban, with PP containers, aluminium foil, paper cups, and other compliant materials widely available at competitive prices.
