Food Wrapping Paper for Street Food: Types, Safety, and Wholesale Buying Guide

April 14, 2025 11 min read Cutlery

India's street food industry is massive -- estimated at over Rs 1.5 lakh crore and growing. From golgappe stalls in Delhi to vada pav vendors in Mumbai, frankie rolls in Pune to kachori shops in Kota, street food is an integral part of Indian culture. And behind every great street food experience is a humble but critical element: wrapping paper.

The right food wrapping paper keeps food warm, absorbs excess oil, prevents leaks, and presents the food attractively. The wrong paper -- or worse, newspaper (which is unfortunately still common) -- can contaminate food with harmful ink chemicals and compromise food safety. This guide covers everything street food vendors, quick-service restaurants, and takeaway businesses need to know about food wrapping paper.

Why Newspaper is Not Food Wrapping Paper

Let us address the elephant in the room. Despite FSSAI advisories against it, newspaper is still widely used for wrapping food in India. Here is why you should stop immediately:

  • Toxic inks: Newspaper inks contain lead, cadmium, and other heavy metals that migrate into food, especially hot and oily food.
  • Bacterial contamination: Newspapers pass through many hands and storage conditions before reaching a food stall. They harbor bacteria, dust, and allergens.
  • FSSAI violation: The Food Safety and Standards Authority of India has explicitly warned against using newspaper for food wrapping. Non-compliance can result in penalties during inspections.
  • Customer perception: Increasingly, urban customers associate newspaper wrapping with poor hygiene. It can cost you repeat business.

The good news is that food-grade wrapping paper is affordable and widely available. There is no valid reason to continue using newspaper in 2025.

Types of Food Wrapping Paper

1. Butter Paper (Greaseproof Paper)

Semi-transparent, smooth paper that resists grease penetration. Ideal for wrapping sandwiches, rolls, burgers, and fried items. It keeps oil contained while allowing the food to "breathe" slightly, preventing excessive sogginess. Butter paper is the workhorse of modern street food wrapping.

2. Parchment Paper (Baking Paper)

Silicone-coated paper that is heat-resistant up to 220 degrees Celsius. Originally designed for baking, it is increasingly used by street food vendors for wrapping hot items like shawarma, frankie rolls, and grilled sandwiches. Its non-stick surface makes it particularly useful for cheese-heavy or sauce-heavy items. Slightly more expensive than butter paper but superior for hot, greasy foods.

3. Wax Paper

Paper coated with a thin layer of food-grade wax (typically paraffin or soy wax). It provides excellent moisture resistance and is good for wrapping cold items, sandwiches, and dry snacks. It should not be used with very hot foods as the wax coating can melt. Available in white and printed varieties.

4. Kraft Paper (Brown Paper)

Unbleached, sturdy brown paper made from wood pulp. It is strong, tear-resistant, and gives a rustic, artisanal look that many modern street food brands prefer. Kraft paper is not inherently greaseproof, but food-grade versions with a light coating are available. It works well for dry items, wrapping around containers, and as a tray liner.

5. Printed Food Wrapping Paper

Custom-printed paper with your brand name, logo, or design. This turns every wrapped item into a marketing opportunity. Printed food wrapping paper is increasingly popular with franchised street food brands and cloud kitchens that want consistent branding on takeaway items. The print must use food-safe, vegetable-based inks.

6. Aluminum Foil-Lined Paper

Paper backed with a thin aluminum layer. It provides excellent heat retention and moisture barrier properties. Used primarily for wrapping shawarma, hot dogs, and items that need to stay warm during delivery. We cover aluminum foil in detail in our aluminum foil packaging guide.

Food Safety Standards for Wrapping Paper

Not all paper labeled "food grade" actually meets food safety standards. Here is what to look for:

  • FSSAI compliance: The paper should be approved for direct food contact under FSSAI regulations.
  • BIS standard IS 6615: This Indian Standard specifies requirements for greaseproof papers for food packaging.
  • No recycled content in food-contact layer: Recycled paper may contain contaminants from its previous life. The food-contact surface must be virgin fiber.
  • Food-safe inks: If the paper is printed, inks must be vegetable-based or otherwise certified as food-safe. Conventional printing inks contain solvents and heavy metals that are harmful when ingested.
  • Fluorochemical-free: Some greaseproof papers use PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) for grease resistance. These "forever chemicals" are increasingly being scrutinized globally. Look for PFAS-free alternatives.

Ask Your Supplier for Documentation

A reliable food wrapping paper supplier should be able to provide an FSSAI compliance certificate and a test report showing the paper is safe for direct food contact. Best Practices for Street Food Vendors

Wrapping Technique Matters

How you wrap is almost as important as what you wrap with:

  • Leave a vent: For hot, crispy items like samosas and pakoras, do not wrap too tightly. Leave a small opening or fold for steam to escape. This prevents the food from getting soggy inside the wrapper.
  • Double wrap oily items: For extremely oily items, use a layer of butter paper directly against the food, then wrap with kraft paper for structural support and a clean exterior.
  • Pre-cut for speed: During busy hours, pre-cut your wrapping paper into standard sizes. A well-organized wrapping station saves seconds per order, which adds up to hours over a day.
  • Keep paper within reach: Store wrapping paper in a clean, dry, covered container right at your serving station. Exposed paper collects dust and debris quickly in outdoor environments.

Portion Sizing

Match your paper size to your food portions to minimize waste:

  • Small items (samosa, tikki): 20 x 20 cm sheets
  • Medium items (sandwich, burger, vada pav): 25 x 30 cm sheets
  • Large items (shawarma, roll, frankie): 30 x 35 cm sheets
  • Extra-large (dosa, large wraps): 35 x 40 cm sheets or larger

The Branding Opportunity

Every piece of wrapping paper is a tiny billboard. Street food brands that invest in custom-printed wrapping paper build recognition faster. Think about it -- a customer walking through a market eating your roll wrapped in branded paper is essentially a walking advertisement.

Custom printing becomes cost-effective at orders of 5,000+ sheets or 50+ rolls. The per-unit premium for printing is typically Rs 0.20-0.50 per sheet -- a small price for constant brand visibility.

Even without custom printing, using clean white or kraft paper instead of newspaper sends a message about your food quality standards. Pair good wrapping with quality tissue napkins and proper disposable cutlery, and you create a complete, professional takeaway experience that customers remember and return to.

Sourcing and Storage

Buy food wrapping paper from specialized food packaging suppliers, not general stationery shops. General-purpose paper may not meet food safety standards. Success Marketing supplies a complete range of food-grade wrapping papers suitable for street food, restaurants, and cloud kitchens.

For storage:

  • Keep in a dry, clean area away from cooking stations (heat and steam damage paper)
  • Store off the floor on shelves or pallets
  • Seal opened packages to prevent dust and pest contamination
  • Rotate stock -- paper has a long shelf life but can yellow or absorb odors over time

The transition from newspaper and random packaging to proper food wrapping paper is one of the simplest upgrades a street food business can make. It improves food safety, customer perception, and regulatory compliance -- all at a cost of just a few paisa per serving.
