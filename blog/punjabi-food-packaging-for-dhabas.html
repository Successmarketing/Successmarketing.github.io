Punjabi Food Packaging for Dhabas and Restaurants

February 18, 2025 13 min read Food Packaging

The Indian highway dhaba is, in many ways, a Punjabi invention. Those roadside eateries with string charpoys, rumali rotis the size of tablecloths, and butter chicken simmering in cauldrons big enough to bathe in have defined how an entire nation eats while travelling. And now, with the explosion of food delivery, the dhaba experience is being packaged and sent to doorsteps across the country.

But there is a fundamental tension in packaging Punjabi food. This is cuisine built for abundance, for large portions served on oversized plates with generous ladles of ghee and butter. Squeezing that experience into a delivery container without losing the soul of the food is the challenge every Punjabi restaurant and dhaba faces today.

We have worked with dhaba owners and Punjabi restaurant chains across Rajasthan's highway belt, from the NH-48 dhabas near Udaipur to the busy truck stops on the Kota-Jaipur highway. Here is what we have learned about packaging Punjabi food right.

The Punjabi Food Packaging Challenge

Punjabi cuisine is characterised by three things that make packaging difficult: richness, volume, and temperature sensitivity.

The Butter and Cream Problem

Butter chicken, dal makhani, paneer butter masala, shahi paneer, malai kofta: the list of Punjabi dishes that swim in butter, cream, and oil is long. This richness is what makes the food irresistible, but it also means your containers need to handle substantial amounts of hot fat without leaking, warping, or becoming greasy on the outside.

Cheap, thin-walled containers are a recipe for disaster with Punjabi food. The hot butter weakens plastic seams within minutes. Aluminium containers without properly sealed lids allow the oily gravy to seep out at every tilt. And paper containers without adequate PE lining become transparent and soggy before the delivery partner even reaches the customer.

The Portion Size Reality

Punjabi food portions are large by any standard. A "half" portion of butter chicken at most North Indian restaurants contains 300-400 ml of gravy with 200-250 grams of chicken. A full portion doubles that. Dal makhani is typically served in 300-500 ml portions. And then there are the breads: two to four naans or rotis per person, each one substantial enough to require its own space.

This means you need larger containers than what most other regional cuisines require. A 500 ml container that works for a South Indian sambar is woefully inadequate for a standard portion of Punjabi gravy.

Container Guide for Punjabi Dishes

Butter Chicken and Gravy Curries

For Punjabi gravy dishes, aluminium foil containers in the 500-750 ml range are the workhorses. Aluminium handles the high temperature of freshly cooked gravy, retains heat for 45-60 minutes, and does not react with the tomato and cream base of most Punjabi gravies. The container should be at least 6-7 cm deep to prevent spillage when the delivery bag tilts.

For premium packaging, PP containers with secure lids offer a cleaner presentation. The semi-transparent lid lets the customer see the rich, orange-red gravy, which builds appetite appeal. However, PP containers do not retain heat as well as aluminium, so they work best when delivery time is under 30 minutes.

A critical detail: always pack the gravy with the meat or paneer pieces submerged. Pieces that sit above the gravy line dry out and look unappetizing by the time they reach the customer. Fill the container so the gravy covers everything, with about a centimetre of headroom below the lid.

Dal Makhani Packaging

Dal makhani is the pride of every Punjabi kitchen, and it is one of the easier dishes to package well. The thick, creamy consistency means it does not slosh around like thinner dals. A 400-500 ml leak-proof container is sufficient for a single serving. The main concern is heat retention, as dal makhani's flavour profile changes noticeably as it cools. The butter solidifies, the cream separates slightly, and the dish loses its characteristic silky texture.

Aluminium containers with cardboard lids provide the best heat retention. If you add a thin layer of tempered butter on top just before sealing, it creates a natural insulation layer that keeps the dal hotter for longer, a trick used by many experienced dhaba owners.

Naan and Roti Packaging

Bread packaging is where most Punjabi restaurants lose the delivery battle. A tandoori naan that comes out of the clay oven soft, pillowy, and slightly charred becomes a stiff, chewy disappointment within 20 minutes if packaged poorly.

The key insight: naans need to breathe, but not too much. A completely sealed container traps steam and makes the naan soggy. A completely open package lets all the heat and moisture escape, turning the naan dry and rigid. The sweet spot is wrapping naans in aluminium foil, which retains heat while allowing just enough moisture to escape through the foil's imperfect seal.

For restaurants packaging multiple naans, wrap each pair in a separate foil sheet rather than wrapping all four or six together. A tall stack of naans in a single foil wrap creates a steam chamber in the centre, making the inner naans soggy while the outer ones dry out. Two naans per foil wrap maintains consistent quality.

For a premium touch, wrap the foiled naans in a paper sleeve or place them in a flat paper box to prevent crushing during transit.

Chole Bhature Packaging

Chole bhature is perhaps the most delivery-hostile Punjabi dish. The bhature are deep-fried, puffed breads that begin deflating the moment they leave the oil. Within ten minutes, a bhatura goes from a magnificent balloon to a flat, oily disc. No packaging in the world can prevent this deflation, but good packaging can at least preserve the texture.

Pack bhature separately from the chole. Use a container or paper wrap that allows steam to escape, preventing the bhature from becoming soggy in their own steam. Some restaurants use perforated paper bags or boxes with ventilation holes specifically for fried breads.

The chole should go in a leak-proof container in the 300-400 ml range. Chole gravy is thinner than most Punjabi curries, so leak-proofing is especially important. Pack the onion-chilli garnish and pickle separately in small sauce cups.

Tandoori Items: Chicken, Paneer Tikka, Seekh Kebab

Tandoori items are dry preparations that need packaging which prevents them from drying out further while avoiding steam buildup that makes them rubbery. This is a delicate balance.

Aluminium foil containers lined with a sheet of butter paper work well. The aluminium retains heat, and the butter paper prevents the tandoori items from sticking to the container. For tandoori chicken, use a container large enough that the pieces do not pile up excessively, as stacking tandoori pieces traps steam between them.

Always include the mint chutney and onion rings in separate small containers. These accompaniments are essential to the tandoori experience, and mixing them with the hot tandoori items in the same container makes the chutney warm and the onions wilted.

Lassi and Beverage Packaging

No Punjabi meal is complete without lassi, and the packaging requirements for this thick yogurt drink are specific. Lassi is heavier and thicker than water or juice, which means the cup needs to support more weight without flexing or crushing. Standard thin paper cups are inadequate for lassi above 200 ml. Use heavy-duty paper cups or PP cups with dome lids for lassi service.

For sweet lassi, the standard sizes are:

  • Small (regular): 250 ml cup
  • Medium (standard at most dhabas): 350-400 ml cup
  • Large (Punjabi size): 500 ml cup

For delivery, cups must have secure lids. Snap-fit dome lids work well for dine-in takeaway, but for delivery through apps, consider using cups with flat lids and a seal strip. This prevents spills during the inevitable tilting and jostling of the delivery bag.

Highway Dhaba Packaging

Highway dhabas have different packaging needs than urban restaurants. Most dhaba takeaway is for travellers who will eat in their car or at a rest stop within 30-60 minutes. The focus is on sturdiness and leak-proofing rather than presentation.

A typical highway dhaba parcel order includes:

For dhabas handling high volumes, especially during meal hours when trucks and travellers queue up, speed of packaging is as important as quality. Containers that stack neatly, lids that snap on quickly, and bags that open easily save precious seconds per order that add up to hours over a busy day.

Punjabi Catering and Wedding Packaging

Punjabi weddings are legendary for their food. The roka, engagement, mehendi, sangeet, wedding, and reception each have their own food service, and the scale can range from 200 to 2,000 guests per event. Disposable packaging is the practical choice for most of these events.

For Punjabi wedding catering, the key items are:

  • Heavy-duty plates: Punjabi wedding food is heavy. A loaded plate with chole, paneer, biryani, naan, and accompaniments can easily weigh 700-800 grams. Plates must handle this without bending or breaking. Our heavy-duty plate range is built for exactly this level of use.
  • Large serving bowls: For the chaats, raita, and dal that are part of every Punjabi wedding menu. Sturdy disposable bowls in the 200-300 ml range.
  • Dessert cups: For kheer, gulab jamun, or phirni. Small cups in the 100-150 ml range with lids.
  • Large aluminium trays: For chafing dish setups in buffet lines. 3-5 litre capacity trays that fit standard chafer frames.

Packaging for Punjabi Cloud Kitchens

The cloud kitchen model has been particularly successful with Punjabi food because the cuisine travels well if packaged correctly and has broad national appeal. Cloud kitchens in cities like Delhi, Mumbai, Bangalore, and Jaipur running Punjabi menus need standardised packaging that works across their entire menu while maintaining brand consistency.

The most efficient approach is to select three to four container sizes that cover your entire menu:

  • A 500 ml container for single-serve gravies and dals
  • A 750-1000 ml container for family-size gravies and rice dishes
  • A flat container or foil wrap for breads
  • Small containers (50-100 ml) for accompaniments

By standardising on a few sizes, you reduce inventory complexity, train staff faster, and negotiate better wholesale prices. Add branded stickers or printed sleeves to create brand identity without the cost of custom-printed containers.

Sustainable Packaging Options for Punjabi Restaurants

With increasing consumer awareness and regulatory pressure around plastic waste, many Punjabi restaurants are exploring sustainable packaging options. Sugarcane bagasse containers, paper-based boxes, and biodegradable plates are becoming viable alternatives, though they come with trade-offs.

Bagasse containers handle moderate temperatures well and work for dry items like tandoori preparations and breads. However, they struggle with very hot, oily gravies like butter chicken, where the oil can seep through over extended periods. For gravy dishes, PP containers remain the most practical choice even for sustainability-minded businesses, as PP is fully recyclable.
